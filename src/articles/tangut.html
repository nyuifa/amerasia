Tangut

The Western Xia or the kingdom of Xi Xia was also known to the Mongols as the Tangut Empire (as well as the Tangut people, an ethnicity), an empire in northwestern China in the eleventh and twelfth centuries. Marco Polo dedicated much attention to the province of Tangut; the cities of Etzina and Sinja, and the province of Su-Chau, all described by Polo, are located on the Vopel Map to north and east of the toponym Tangut (Ezina, Gingui, and Suchur). In Etzina, “The inhabitants are idolaters and they take pleasure in the camels and the untamed wild asses” (Incolae idolatrae came/lis et onagris syluestri/bus gaudent). In Sinja, he says that “The inhabitants worship idols. Having an abundance of silk, they make elegant cloths from the barks of trees” (Incolae Idola colunt. serici copiam/ habentes, faciuntque pannos elegan/tes de arborum corticibus). In Su-Chau, Vopel says, “The inhabitants are in part Christian and in part idolatrous, subject to the Great Khan, and they have both cities and towns” (Incolae partim Christiani partim/ Idolatrae, subiecti magno Cham/ ciuitates et oppida habentes). Further north and east, Vopel also includes the Kingdom of Goch, also in Tangut (link to Goch entry).

Tangut was a toponym present on many Amerasian maps (link to Wu essay). Vopel asserts “here the best musk is found” (Hic muschum optimum/ inuenitur), repeating Polo’s claim that “this country produces the best and finest musk in the world,” and alluding once again to the Asian riches that could be reached in the exploration of the Americas.[1] (Vopel also discusses the musk of beavers in his cartouche about Calicut, on the right edge of the map, in the Indian Ocean.) The Tangut Empire occupied a stretch of the Silk Road, and its wealth clearly attracted Vopel’s attention, as we can see by the copious number of cities and their wares from this part of China, such as silk and camels, that Vopel decided to include and describe on his map. Describing the city of Kalachan—not included on Vopel’s map, but noted by Polo in the province of Tangut—Polo states for instance that “in this city the finest camlets in the world are made of camel hair.”[2] Camlet was a luxurious fabric traditionally made from a combination of silk and camel hair. After hearing Fra Marcos de Niza’s description of his 1539 expedition along the upper Rio Grande in what is today New Mexico, Rodrigo de Albornoz, the treasurer of New Spain, wrote a summary of de Niza’s journey for the royal treasurer in Spain on 18 October 1539 reporting that de Niza had visited a land populated by camels, elephants, and people wearing camlet. It is likely that Albornoz was seeing Tangut in de Niza’s description of these regions north and west of Mexico.[3]

East of Tangut on Vopel’s map is Tamacho. Here, Vopel tells us “Once called Tanguth, here they make precious garments and fine linen” (Olim Tanguth dicta, faciunt/ hic pannos praeciosos adque [sic]/ sindones.) Vopel derived this idea—that Tamacho (a Mexican toponym) was once called Tangut—from the work of the Belgian cartographer Franciscus Monachus, who claimed in his De orbis situ (Antwerp, 1527) that “North of Culvacania spreads Thamacho, formerly called Tangut.”[4] (There are several instances in Vopel’s map of a clear reliance on Monachus; see, for example the cartouche: America connected to the eastern lands [link to cartouche]. For Monachus and after him Vopel and Vavassore, China and Mexico overlapped, both temporally and spatially.
