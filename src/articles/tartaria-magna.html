Tartaria Magna

Great Tartary was historically a territory located in northern and central Asia populated by Turkic peoples and Mongols; it stretched from the Caspian to the Pacific. It was often divided into regions held by different ruling powers, such as Muscovite Tartary, Cathay Tartary (Xinjiang and Mongolia, indicated on Vopel’s map just to the east of Tartaria Magna).

One tribe of the Mongol confederacy bore the name Tatar, a word that for some Europeans recalled Tartarus, the hell of classical mythology. Especially after the passage of Mongol armies laid waste large parts of Central Asia in the middle of the thirteenth century, it became commonplace for the term Tatar or Tartar to stand in for the Mongols in general, as Europeans began referring to them as a kind of generic diabolical horde.[1] When Polo uses the term Tartar, which he does regularly throughout his text, he is referring to the Mongols.

The Tartars sometimes figured in Amerasian understandings of the world, often through rhetorics of similarity that likened them to New World peoples. For instance, In Michele de Cuneo’s letter to Hieronymo Annari, written in October of 1495 and describing Columbus’s second voyage in which Cuneo took part, Cuneo reported that the faces of Caribbean peoples “resemble a Tartar’s.”[2] Similarly, the English explorer Martin Frobisher described the Inuit as “like Tartars.”[3] Cornelis de Jode’s regional map of the Northwest Coast of America, Quivirae Regnu cum alijs versus Borea, from the second edition of his atlas Speculum Orbis Terrae (Antwerp, 1593), similarly equated the tents of the local population to those of the Tartars.

In addition to such discussions about the similarities between Tartars and New World peoples, many in the New World imagined that the Tartars were not far away. For instance, the chronicler Fray Diego de Ocaña described a civic ritual that took place in Potosí in 1601 in which a figure dressed as the devil threw printed broadsheets to the crowd that proclaimed “the Prince of Tartary, who of sulphur/sustains himself in the dark cavern,/will present himself at half past five,/and he thanks those who shall suffer until then.”[4] While Potosiani may not have literally believed that the Prince of Tartary was hiding in a cave in a nearby valley, they nevertheless felt his presence. In his New English Canaan (1637) which described the indigenous peoples of Massachusetts, the colonist Thomas Morton acknowledged that “what part of this mane continente may be thought to border upon the country of the Tartars it is yet unknowne.”[5] In Morton’s imaginary, the new pilgrim colonies in New England were founded on territory that might well extend into Great Tartary, an idea that was slow to die. Colonial writers such as James Adair and Jonathan Carver continued to discuss whether or not indigenous American tribes like the Chickasaw were descended from the Tartars until the end of the eighteenth century.[6]
