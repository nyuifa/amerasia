Chatay

As Peiyue Wu’s essay “Tangut, Cathay, and Mangi on Sixteenth-Century World Maps” discusses at length (link to WU essay), ideas about Cathay (northern China, but often intended to mean China itself) were at the center of Amerasian understandings of the world; a great variety of sixteenth-century world maps showed its location as proximate to Mexico. In 1493, Columbus described the lands he had reached “in the province of Cathay” (la provincia de Catayo).[1] Around the end of the fifteenth century, John Cabot was convinced that during his voyages along the coast of North America that “if I should sail by way of the Northwest, I should by a shorter tract come into India…not thinking to find any other land that that of Cathay.”[2] Peter Martyr reported that when Vicente Yáñez and Pinzón and his nephew Arias Pinzón landed in Brazil and sailed along the coast of Paria in 1500, they believed they were “beyond the city of Cathay.”[3] The Italian explorer Giovanni da Verrazano similarly stated in the 1520s during his voyages to North America that his intention was “to reach Cathay and the extreme eastern coast of Asia.”[4]

While many explorers like these expressed the desire to find Cathay in the Americas, Cathay also became a symbol of Asian wealth that was regularly folded into early descriptions of these new lands to the west of the Atlantic. For instance, describing Francisco Vázquez de Coronado’s expedition into what is today the American Southwest, Francisco López de Gómara recounted in his Historia general de las Indias that when the Coronado party reached Quivira, in what is today Kansas, “they saw on the coast ships which had pelicans of gold and silver on their prows, with merchandise that they thought to be from Cathay and China, because they made signs that they had sailed thirty days.”[5] Later in the century, when the English explorer Martin Frobisher returned from his search for a northwest passage in the 1570s, auditors investigating the financial failure of Frobisher's voyages described an Eskimo captive Frobisher brought back to England as a “strange man of Cathay." The Flemish painter Cornelis Ketel was commissioned to make portraits of the man (none of which have survived), and Frobisher claimed reimbursement for the "apparell for the strainge man of Cathay or new land India."[6]
