Draconis

Off the coast of what is today Venezuela, Vopel has labeled the sea DRACONIS, referring to Columbus’s Boca del Drago, or “Mouth of the Dragon” to describe the tumultuous seas that churned around the delta of the mighty Orinoco River. During his third voyage in 1498, Columbus stood near this spot and reasoned that such a mighty river implied a great height inland, certainly the earthly paradise at the easternmost part of the earth.[1] Similarly, in the so-called Mundus novus letter, published in 1502 and purportedly authored by Amerigo Vespucci, Vespucci described the lands he visited in the western Atlantic, claiming that “certainly, if anywhere in the world there exists an Earthly Paradise, I think it is not far from these regions.”[2]

Medieval historians, cosmographers, and cartographers regularly placed the Terrestrial Paradise somewhere in the East. John Mandeville for instance, who offered one of the most detailed descriptions of Paradise (and one that Columbus likely read) described it as an island “far to the east, beyond…the land of Prester John…at the very beginning of the world.”[3] Mandeville reported having heard that Paradise was the highest region in the world, enclosed by a wall, with a spring discharging the Ganges, Nile, Tigris and Euphrates from its center.[4] Medieval mappae mundi also located Paradise in the East.[5]

These medieval understandings of directly informed how early modern explorers saw the Americas, who saw Asian lands in their voyages to the New World. If Paradise was in the East, and these explorers believed themselves to be close to its location, their understanding of the world therefore implied that the Americas overlapped with the lands of the Orient.
