Ophir displaced

Vittoria Riccio

In his Lettera rarissima, written on July 7 1503, Christopher Columbus proclaimed to the King and Queen of Spain, "Gold is most excellent. Gold constitutes treasure; and he who possesses it does all he wishes to in this world and succeeds in helping souls into Paradise."[1] Discussions of gold pervade the so-called Lettera rarissima,as well as the Vopel map, where we see in the region of India Alta, just northeast of the Kingdom of Bengal, the label "Aurea, Eusebius calls [this place] Orphira" (Aurea/Eusebius Orphiram vocat). [2] The relationship between gold and exploration will not surprise the modern reader, yet its association with salvation might at first appear odd. The Admiral, however, was undeniably inspired by a profound religious sentiment; his voyages were not only motivated by a desire for geographical or political expansion, but also by a quest to confirm the Biblical text. The Lettera of 1503 is a fundamental document in the Columbian corpus, not only as a detailed source on his travel, but also as a testimony of the role of sacred scripture in his understanding of the world. The last section of this letter, in fact, is dedicated to the mythical land of Ophir and the gold of King Solomon, illuminating the nature of a mission that fused together a quest for riches, exploration, and salvific benefits. While Ophir shows up on Vopel"s map in its most eastern realms, its relevance trickles over to the Amerasian continent in terms of sixteenth-century perceptions of its meaning. From the annihilation of the great Central American empires to the emergence of myths and tales on golden men and cities, the European conception of the New World was inevitably shaped by visions of gold, which were embodied by Ophir and those who sought out this mythical location in the Americas.

Famous for its wealth, Ophir was the region from which King Solomon received a cargo of gold, silver, sandalwood, pearls, ivory, apes and peacocks every three years (1 Kings 9:28 and 10:22). In the first quarter of the sixteenth century, European cosmographers began to identify Ophir with Hispaniola (link to Hispaniola entry). Jewish tradition in particular often associated Ophir with a place in India, and it was surely this Indian location that allowed Ophir"s placement to elide into the Americas.[3] As we shall see, generations of cosmographers and antiquarians continued to try to determine Ophir"s exact location, well into the early modern period, placing it at times in Africa, India and the Americas, and often in Peru.[4]

The Hebrew Bible associated Ophir and Tarsis (Tharsis, Tarshish) (link to THARSIS entry). They are both mentioned in the Genesis as the progeny of Noah: Ophir, the eleventh of Ioctan"s thirteen sons, descends from Shem (Gen. 10:29 et seq.) and Tarsis, son of Iavan, from Iafet (Gen. 10:4; Chron. 1:7).[5] Ophir is also referred to as a Ioctanite or South-Arabian tribe. These names are found again in the Book of Kings (9:26 et seq.) and in Chronicles (8:17 et seq.) as places of incredible wealth and sources of precious metals, gems and exotic animals. According to the scriptures, King David donated 3,000 talents of Ophir gold for the construction of the Temple in Jerusalem. Later, his son Solomon and King Hiram of Tyro sent their ships to these legendary mines; their fleet sailed every three years from the port of Ezion-Geber in the Red Sea, bringing back to Jerusalem 420 talents of gold[6] (I Kings 9:26-28), silver, ivory, apes, peacocks (I Kings 10:22), precious stones and a special variety of wood called almug (I Kings 10:12; II Chron. 2:8; 9:10-11). A hundred years later, King Jehoshaphat"s attempt to reach the golden land of Ophir resulted in failure, his ships wrecked at Ezion-Geber before they could even sail to sea.

The "gold of Ophir" is generally gold of the finest quality, as we read, for instance, in the Book of Job: "It cannot be bought with pure gold; Nor can silver be weighed out in exchange for it. It cannot be bought with gold of Ophir; Nor with rare onyx and sapphire" (Job 28:15, 16). In 1946, a potsherd bearing the inscription "Ophir gold to Bet Horon, thirty shekels" was unearthed in Tell el-Qasileh, north of Tel Aviv, a discovery that demonstrates the wide-ranging association between Ophir and gold, as well as a common knowledge of these lands and their trades with Jerusalem.[7] The case of Tarsis is not dissimilar. Tarsis was Jonah"s destination in his attempted escape from divine commands (Jonah 1:3) and it was known to be a maritime port in the extreme regions of the earth. The idea of remoteness implied in Jonah"s story and the association with trade are possibly the basis on which a new meaning for the word developed: in many Biblical passages, "ships of Tarsis" are vessels equipped for long-distance travels, not necessarily connected to the city anymore.

Fabled lands of gold, silver and precious gems are not exclusive to the Old Testament; they have populated human imaginary, folklore and epic traditions, throughout the centuries. The first allusion to a golden land appears as early as the fourth century B.C., in the Indian epic poem Ramayana, where we read of the marvelous island and land of gold, respectively called Suvarnadvipa and Suvarnabhumi. In Chinese ancient literature, we find mentions of a magnificent region of gold known as Jin-Lin, the "Golden Neighbor" or "Golden Border". In the majority of cases, these fantastic and opulent lands were located in Sumatra, Java or Malaysia and in rare instances further East, probably in the area of modern Philippines. The Ancient Greek Chryse and Argyve, the island of gold and its silver pendant, were thought to be not far from India, somewhere at the mouth of the Ganges or beyond the river. In the first century A.D., these two legendary islands are mentioned in authors such as Pomponius Mela and Marinos of Tyro, as well as in the Periplus Mari Erythraei, an anonymous treatise describing navigation routes and trades in the Red Sea and in the Indian Ocean. In his Naturalis Historia, Pliny the Elder discusses the supposed location of the islands, this time situated at the mouth of the Indus River.

Beyond the mouth of the Indus are the islands of Chryse and Argyre, abounding in metals, I believe; but as to what some persons have stated, that their soil consists of gold and silver, I am not so willing to believe that.[8]

Chryse and Argyve–and their Indian location–are also part of Herodotus" Historiae and Josephus" Antiquitates Judaicae, where for the first time an identification between Ophir and Chryse–here called Aurea–is proposed. Finally, Ptolemy in his Geography locates Chryse and Argyve beyond the Chersonesus Aurea ("Golden peninsula"), identified with the present-day Malaysian peninsula. The Western imagination clearly located incredible Oriental wealth on the islands of Southeast Asia. (link to ARCHIPELAGUM entry)

Such thought directly influenced Europeans" understanding of the lands discovered in the western Atlantic. As we saw, Ancient Greek and Roman writers tended to associate gold and abundance with India; whether it was in relation to Chryse or to the Golden Chersonese, the idea of distance was connected to that of wealth. Herodotus writes:

It seems as if the extreme regions of the earth were blessed by nature with the most excellent productions, just in the same way that Hellas enjoys a climate more excellently tempered than any other country. In India, which, as I observed lately, is the furthest region of the inhabited world towards the east, all the four-footed beasts and the birds are very much bigger than those found elsewhere, except only the horses, which are surpassed by the Median breed called the Nisaean. Gold too is produced there in vast abundance, some dug from the earth, some washed down by the rivers, some carried off in the mode which I have but now described. And further, there are trees which grow wild there, the fruit whereof is a wool exceeding in beauty and goodness that of sheep. The natives make their clothes of this tree-wool.[9]

Early Christian and Jewish authors, such as Josephus or Jerome, embraced this existing parallel and applied it to biblical exegesis, generating a new current of interpretation that was still alive in the early modern era. An important element in the identification India-Ophir is philology, a tool that also proved to be critical in the New World debate. In the Septuagint and in early commentators, the name of the golden city of Solomon is sometimes transcribed as Sophir or Souphir, terms that were immediately compared with the Indian city of Souppara described by Ptolemy and the Coptic name for India, Sophir. The profusion of gold, silver and gems in the region as well as the presence of animals such as apes and elephants, described in the bible, provided a further level of confirmation and the mysterious almug wood was interpreted as Indian sandalwood.

When Columbus sailed West, such notions were well ingrained in his thought, and he left Spain in the hopes of reaching Ophir by another route. On the globe created by Martin Behaim in 1492, Ophir is depicted as an island at the mouth of the Ganges. The globe was likely known to the Admiral, and his interest in Ophir is evident in his writings and annotations to authors such as Josephus and Pierre d"Ailly. As mentioned, before Columbus, Ophir and Tarsis were considered separate entities. Tarsis was generally associated with the ancient city of Tarsus in Asia Minor or with the Spanish port of Tartessus, near Cadiz; alternative theories sought for the biblical port in Sardinia, Britain, and Africa, but the idea of an Eastern–and by extension American–placement arose only after the first voyage in 1492. In the Libro de las profecias, edited in collaboration with his son Ferdinando and fray Gaspar the Gorricio in 1501- 1503, Columbus develops the assimilation between the two lands, asserting that Tarsis is an island, just like Ophir and Cethim. This last toponym is found again in association with Ophir and Tarsis in a letter to pope Alexander VI from 1502 in which we read: "This island is Tarsis, Cethim, Ophir, Ophaz and Cipangu, and we named it Hispaniola." An undatable note in his personal copy of Pliny"s Naturalis Historia equates the two biblical cities to Hispaniola, Feyti, and Cipangu.

Ideas about climate played a consistent role in these discussions. Tropical temperatures are associated with gold, becoming a common denominator linking India, Indonesia, Malaysia and the Caribbean.[10] On November 14 1492, passing by the shores of Cuba, Columbus noted in his journal: "According to the great heat that I am suffering, this country must be rich in gold."[11] In the Lettera rarissima, Columbus writes:

Josephus thinks that this gold was found in the Aurea; if it were so, I contend that these mines of the Aurea are identical to those of Veragua, which as I have said before, extends westward twenty days" journey, and they are at an equal distance from the Pole and the Line.[12]

Beyond identifying the mines of Ophir with those of Veragua, Columbus also places them at the same latitude, affirming that "they are at an equal distance from the Pole and the Line". A combination of ideas about distance, heat, latitude and biblical exegesis all came together to produce theories of a New World Ophir. In Vopel"s map, we find mentions of abundant gold cluster in the vicinity of Beragua, a deeply tropical location just north of the equator on the mainland west of Hispaniola. Just east of the name Beragua, an inscription refers to rivers carrying gold, and just to the west another inscription informs us that along this entire coast much gold is to be found. Just off the eastern coast of this isthmus, we read of happy Jamaica, rich in gold and gems, all pointing to golden, if not Ophirian associations with this part of the world.

Later in the century, Benito Arias Montano, a learned Spanish scholar at the court of Philip II, further articulated the connection between Ophir and the Americas by proposing a theory about the true location of Ophir in his text Phaleg. According to Montano, Ophir (here, a person as well as a place) and his brother Iobab, sons of Ioctan, arrived in the New World to spread the word of JHWH. Iobab colonized Peru, and Ioctan, Brazil. Montano considered a variety of biblical verses regarding the building of Solomon"s Temple, including "and the gold was the gold of Parvaim" (2 Chronicles 3:6). Drawing on his philological expertise, Montano asserted that the suffix "-im", in the words"Parvaim" "Paruaim" ; "aurum Parvaim" represented a Hebrew dual ending. For Montano, this meant that "Parvaim" meant "double land of Peru"; in effect, 2 Chronicles 3:6 should read "And this was the gold of Peru and Peru".[13] Montano therefore located the two cities of Ophir on the Pacific coast of North and South America. Montano also believed that the descendants of Noah were listed in the Bible in geographical order, from west to east. Ophir came before his brother Iobab, the last one and the easternmost of Noah"s progeny. According to the text, Iobab settled on a mountain called Sepher, which Montano identifies in the Andes, east of Peru. Reminiscences of this biblical colonization, in the end, could be found in toponyms such as the ancient city of Yuctan or the Yucatan peninsula, an homage to the name of their father Ioctan.

Many had critiqued Montano"s ideas, including Jos" de Acosta and Garcilasco de la Vega. However, the Spanish priest and chronicler Gregorio Garc"a, continued to develop Montano"s theories in his Origen de los Indios del Nuevo Mundo e Indias Occidentales (Valencia, 1607). By scrutinizing both physical and linguistic similarities between the indios and Israelites, as well as the persistence of the ancient name Peru in names such as Paraguay, Paria or Piura, Garcia maintained that not only was the idea of a biblical migration to the New Continent correct, but that the Peruvian location for the mythical Ophir was its indisputable consequence.[14] When the Spanish navigator Alvaro Menda"a became the first European to land in the Solomon Islands in 1568, he named them as such because he believed them to be the location of Ophir.[15]

While piety may have motivated Columbus to search for Ophir, the forces of Spanish imperialism also played a role in Europeans" desire to find this ancient place. In either case, any claim that the indios inhabiting the Americas were descendants of the Israelites was a double-edged sword. On one hand, they could have been seen as offspring of a chosen people and therefore worthy of salvation; on the other hand, they could have been accused of having renounced the faith and instead preferred the path of vice and barbarism. However applied, theories about the location of Ophir became  tools in the hands of European powers. The Habsburgs could use it to sustain their claims on the people, lands and riches of America: if the indios descended from Noah or from one of the ten lost tribes of Israel, they arrived in the New World through the Iberian Peninsula, establishing a relation that went beyond any treatise or papal bull.[16] Besides political and expansionistic drives, however, the case of Ophir represents a critical instance of the shaping of a new global vision after the discovery of America. The fusion of different traditions, secular and religious, ancient and modern, and the overlapping of different geographical spheres is an important reminder of the fluidity of the world in the sixteenth century.
