Prester John and The Search for a Christian Utopia

Larimore Hampton Pivar

The coinage of the city of Axum proves the acceptance of Christianity in Ethiopia in the third and fourth centuries.  Although informed by trade-related interests, the decision to embrace Christianity laid the foundation for the myth of Prester John.[1] The kingdom of Axum was vanquished by the 12th century and the seat of power moved to the new capital at Roha, now Lalibela. It is the reign of Lalibela that is most frequently identified with the Prester John story.

Prester John’s existence was first recorded in the literature of the 12th century, although there had been rumors of sightings centuries before Otto of Freising documented the fable of Prester John in his Chronica sice Historia de duabus civitatibus of 1145, where we are told of a rumor of “a certain John, a king and priest who dwells beyond Persia and Armenia in the uttermost East. . . . It is said that he is a lineal descendant of the Magi, of whom mention is made in the Gospel.”[2]  These rumors fed the desire for more concrete evidence, soon to come in a [supposed] letter from Prester John to the Pope in Rome, from 1165. The many adaptations of the Letter advanced Prester John’s legend, according to which he was “ruling in ‘India,’ a geographical expression that to Europeans in 1165 and later meant nothing more specific than a land lying to the east.”[3] The Letter further referred to his domain extending over ‘three Indies,’ which in the European vernacular of the period referred to a vague tripartite categorization of the land masses beyond the Arab world into Lesser, Greater, and Middle India. Likewise, the Ethiopian people would be designated Indians.[4]

With increasing European trade with Cathay and the possibility of making a pact with the Mongols in the fight against the Muslims, the Letter spurred on Marco Polo’s quest in the East from 1271 to 1295. All of the accounts in the literature portray Prester John as a powerful Christian, or Nestorian, king who ruled a land that can only be described as utopian, a land of abundance and wealth and peace. As what often occurs with the synchronicity of the collective consciousness, Prester John is to be found [in the literature] in places associated with the geopolitical and religious zeitgeist. Prester John’s importance to the medieval Christian world is prodigious as it perpetuates the notion that a failing Christendom is not inevitable and that continued expansion of its realm is attainable.[5] In the late fourteenth and fifteenth centuries the dissemination of the legend of Prester John shifted him from Asia to Ethiopia, as a reflection of cartographical amendments to the fusion of eastern Asia and eastern Africa.[6]                                

Columbus was purported to have traveled West with Polo’s Travels, and to have owned The Book of John Mandeville as well as the Letter of Prester John.[7]  When Europe came to understand Columbus’ discoveries, the Letter was adapted into verse by Calabrian priest Giuliano Dati, first published in Rome in 1493 (Figs. 1a, 1b).[8] Either a priest or rimester sang of the tale of Prester John in the streets in ottava rima to the common man; the songs became legendary themselves.[9]

To understand the scope of Prester John’s grip on the European imagination it is necessary not only to read the literature but to consider visual representations. Depictions of Prester John and his realm are ubiquitous early on, most notably in maps, as European cartographers’ dependence on maps was a crucial element in pinpointing the location of Prester John. According to Carlson, any map depicting Africa during the 300 years after Marco Polo was not considered complete without picturing or referencing Prester John.[10] These cartographers appear to locate Prester John in places that produced the least confusion about the changing world. Prester John’s prominence of place on maps was due to a desire to ascribe certainty to the inconstant and mutable worldview. Maps from the Middle Ages had depicted Ethiopia and India as bordering land massed, with the Indian Ocean severely reduced. In the Age of Exploration European cosmographers divided India in two parts, Indus and the Ganges and Beyond the Ganges, with Africa depicted separately.

In a 1450-60 Catalan World Map, by an anonymous Spanish artist (Figs. 2a, 2b),[11] Prester John is located in Africa and is depicted as a dark-skinned ruler sitting on a throne in a tent, surrounded by other rulers of the continent. While clearly not a European, he wears contemporary European clothing and a crown, holds a scepter, and appears very imperial.

In the so-called Genoese Planisphere of 1457 (Fig. 3),[12]  by an anonymous Florentine, Prester John is located in Africa and is depicted as a very dark-skinned Ethiopian Christian ruler wearing a crown and holding aloft a Crusades banner surmounted by a cross.  His costume is more typically Eastern, with his head tied up in a turban that flows down his shoulders and back.

 

Fig. 3

 

Hartmann Schedel’s Nuremberg Chronicle of 1493 (Fig. 4)[13] portrays Prester John as a medieval monk with costumes and furniture in Gothic style. Here Prester John is receiving the eucharist from a seated Christ. Prester John is light-skinned here, possibly an indication of his location, which is said to be India. Whether the artists were aware of it or not, they were portraying the Prester as they saw him in their minds, as European. Here John is clearly not represented as a monarch as is usually found but, as always, as a Christian.

 

Fig. 4

 

 

In Juan de La Cosa’s 1500 Planisphere, (Fig. 5),[14]  Prester John and his kingdom are situated at the top of Africa, just below the fork of the Nile, where he is said to have great powers over the river.  Prester John’s appearance here is again European and light-skinned, but wearing a short robe or a toga, bearing a crown, and holding a staff. He is bare-legged to the knees. This depiction decidedly harkens back to antiquity. He is moving in haste towards the East. 

 

Fig. 5

 

Martin Waldseemüller and Matthias Ringmann’s 1507 Universalis Cosmographia (Fig. 6)[15] was the first map to depict the New World as its own continent, with the labeling of the land “America.” Prester John’s kingdom is placed in the eastern part of the map, in Asia, while most maps of this period placed Prester John in Ethiopia.

Fig. 6

Francisco Alvares’ Ho Preste Joam das Indias, of 1540 (Fig. 7),[16] with drawings by an anonymous Portuguese artist, portrayed the Prester (here located in India) as a nobleman on horseback, heading off into battle with banner raised. The Prester’s features and skin tone are clearly European. Not only is he wearing contemporary European costume, but he is situated outside of [his] castle, which is fashioned of Portuguese white plaster and a red ceramic tile roof, making him appear to be at Iberian Royal Court, living as a European king would live.

Fig. 7

Giovanni Vavassore and Caspar Vopel’s large-scale printed map of 1558 (Fig. 8, detail)[17] depicts Prester John as a Christian ruler in the land of Abassia, as well as Greater India of Ethiopia, further confusing the location of the mythic king.[18] He is here shown as an elderly bearded man with African facial features. He holds a scepter, wears a crown surmounted by a cross, and is enrobed in traditional regal garments. It is clear that Prester John’s physical features, demeanor, and locality are constantly shifting.

Fig. 8

A Spanish Portolan Chart from 1568 here shows Prester John positioned in East Africa [detail] (Fig. 9),[19] even though the map titles Prester John as Preste Iuan de las Indias. He is depicted here as an elderly robed European Christian ruler on a throne, looking off to the West in a wary manner. He holds a scepter topped with a cross and wears an elaborate crown surmounted by a cross and makes the sign of blessing with the fingers of his right hand.  To make the point even stronger that the Prester is a Christian ruler, even the throne chair supports are surmounted by crosses of wood. To his right is a King of Mogadishu (Rei d’ Magadallo), dressed in the attire of a Muslim sultan with red turban and saber, his beard cropped. The skin tones of both Prester and the sultan are very similar---light---with the sultan having Eastern facial features versus Prester’s European features.

Fig. 9

A 1572 Spanish Portolano by Jaimes Olives, one of nine maps (Fig. 10),[20]  shows Eastern Europe, Asia Minor, and North Africa, west of the Nile, with Prester John located west of the Nile.  The artists portray Prester John as if he were the Pope, with mitered hat and scepter, blessing the crowd and facing West. His is the sole image on the map that approximates a European—all other represented rulers are depicted as “exotic.”

Fig. 10

Abraham Ortelius’ map of the land of Prester John, published in Theatrum Orbis Terrarum (Fig. 11),[21] shows that as late as 1585 the legend of the Prester still held sway over the European worldview. There is no depiction of the ruler but that an entire map is titled in his name, and that his coat of arms at the top left shows the Prester’s importance in the mind of the European. Maps of this sort are common, always using the typology (and often image) of Prester John and his kingdom to identify Ethiopia or other parts of Africa.

Fig. 11

In Orla’s 1599 engraving of Prester John we see him depicted as a Christian ruler of Ethiopia (Fig. 12),[22]  looking European in contemporary dress but once again darker-skinned and with African facial features.  Wearing a domed crown surmounted by a cross and wielding a scepter, with a shield of the Crusades hung in the background, he is represented as the civilized and learned Prester John that would be greeted at a visitation to the Pope in Rome.

 

Fig. 12

Nicholas Larmessin’s engraving of Prester John presents him as a very dark-skinned man majestically bejeweled as an Ethiopian emperor (Fig, 13),[23] both accoutered with pearls and brocades and yet still perceived as an “other,” as he is only partially clothed. Despite this, the Prester is given the presence of an aristocrat, looking straight ahead and noble.

Fig. 13

In Manuel D’ Almeida’s frontispiece of Historia geral de Ethiopia a Alta ou Preste Ioam of 1660 (Fig, 14),[24]  the artist portrays Prester John as a dark-skinned ruler of Ethiopia meeting with Jesuit priests, with the Prester clad in Renaissance attire seated atop a base and surrounded by the lushness of a tropical country---tall stylized palm trees, heraldic angels and a curtained backdrop with the frontispiece of the book shown directly behind him. This depiction veers more towards a vision of the Prester John and his realm as part of a new world being approached by foreigners, although it is known that Jesuit priests had long been involved in missionary work in Ethiopia.  Some of the Prester’s attendants appear apprehensive of the European visitors, their eyes seeing “foreigners,” with the European artist viewing the Prester and his realm as the “other.”  The image depicts an odd confluence of confusion, presenting Ethiopia as a New World.
