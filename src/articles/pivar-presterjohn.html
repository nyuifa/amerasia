Prester John and The Search for a Christian Utopia

Larimore Hampton Pivar

In eastern Africa, just above the striped band that Caspar Vopel uses to designate the equator, the mapmaker depicts a stern looking figure bearing a mitre and a crozier: the mythical priest king, Prester John. Though here he sits neither in America nor the Orient, his history has much to tell us about Amerasia.

Vopel here places the king in what was the antique African city-state of Axum, in what is today northern Ethiopia, and local coinage from archaeological digs here have confirmed that Christianity became accepted here in the third and fourth centuries. The decision to embrace Christianity in this part of the world – first introduced here by regional trade routes – helped to lay the foundation for the myth of Prester John.[1] The kingdom of Axum was vanquished by the twelfth century and the local seat of power moved to the new capital at Roha, now Lalibela. It is the reign of Lalibela that is most frequently identified with the Prester John story.

Prester JohnÕs existence was first recorded in the literature of the twelfth century when Otto of Freising told in his Chronica sice Historia de duabus civitatibus (1145) of Òa certain John, a king and priest who dwells beyond Persia and Armenia in the uttermost East [...] It is said that he is a lineal descendant of the Magi, of whom mention is made in the Gospel.Ó[2] These rumors fed the desire for more concrete evidence, soon to come in a [supposed] letter from Prester John to the Pope in Rome, from 1165. The many adaptations of the Letter advanced Prester JohnÕs legend, according to which he was Òruling in Ô India,Õ a geographical expression that to Europeans in 1165 and later meant nothing more specific than a land lying to the east.Ó[3]In the Middle Ages, Europeans also began to designate Ethiopian people as Indians, and we can see that Vopel labels places the label ÒIndiaÓ just to the west of his figure of Prester John.[4] Marco Polo documented Prester John's kingdom in the province of Tangut (see GOCH entry), and John Mandeville similarly described the priest-kingÕs wealthy and marvelous court in the lands in or near Persia.[5] > For Europeans, the legend of Prester John and his kingdom perpetuatedthe notion that Christendom could continue to expand.[6] Indeed, in the late fourteenth and fifteenth centuries, the placement of Prester John shifted from Asia to Ethiopia.[7] Here,we see a geographical melding that prefigured and echoed the Amerasian phenomenon as medieval Europeans merged and associated India and Ethiopia in much the same way early modern Europeans would conflate and combine America and Asia.

A great variety of late medieval and early modern world maps depict Prester John and show his migration from India to Ethiopia. The 1450-60 Catalan World Map (Figs. 1a, 1b), [8] for instance, locates Prester John in Africa, where he is depicted as a dark-skinned ruler sitting on a throne in a tent, surrounded by other rulers of the continent. While clearly not a European, he wears contemporary European clothing and a crown, holds a scepter, and appears very imperial.

The so-called Genoese Planisphere of 1457 (Fig. 2)[9] also places Prester John in Africa, where he is shown as a very dark-skinned Ethiopian Christian ruler wearing a crown and holding aloft a Crusades banner surmounted by a cross. His costume is more typically Eastern, with his head tied up in a turban that flows down his shoulders and back.

Figure 2

Hartmann SchedelÕs Nuremberg Chronicle of 1493 [10] portrays Prester John as a medieval monk with costumes and furniture in Gothic style. Here, the priest-king is receiving the eucharist from a seated Christ. He is light-skinned, possibly an indication of his location, which is said to be India. Whether the artists were aware of it or not, they were portraying the Prester as they saw him in their minds, as European, and represented not so much as a monarch as a Christian.

Figure 3

On the Juan de La Cosa planisphere (1500) (Fig. 4), [11] Prester John and his kingdom are situated at the top of Africa, just below the fork of the Nile, where he is said to have great powers over the river. Prester JohnÕs appearance here is again European and light-skinned, but wearing a short robe or a toga, bearing a crown, and holding a staff. He is bare-legged to the knees. This depiction decidedly harkens back to antiquity. He is moving in haste towards the East.

Figure 4

. Martin Waldseemüller and Matthias RingmannÕs 1507 Universalis Cosmographia (Fig. 5), [12] the first map to depict the New World as its own continent and to label this land ÒAmerica,Ó by contrast puts Prester JohnÕs kingdom back in Asia.

Figure 5

.In the second half of the sixteenth century, the Vopel map (Fig. 6, detail) depicts Prester John as a Christian ruler in the land of Abassia, as well as Greater India of Ethiopia, further confusing the location of the mythic king.[13] Here, he is an elderly bearded man with African facial features. He holds a scepter, wears a crown surmounted by a cross, and is enrobed in traditional regal garments. It is clear that Prester JohnÕs physical features, demeanor, and locality are constantly shifting.

Figure 6

.A Spanish Portolan Chart from 1568 shows Prester John positioned in East Africa [detail] (Fig. 7), [14] even though the map titles Prester John as >Preste Iuan de las Indias, once again signalling the conflation and merging of Ethiopia and India. He is depicted here as an elderly robed European Christian ruler on a throne, looking off to the West in a wary manner. He holds a scepter topped with a cross and wears an elaborate crown surmounted by a cross and makes the sign of blessing with the fingers of his right hand. Even the throne chair supports are surmounted by crosses of wood. To his right is a King of Mogadishu (Rei dÕ Magadallo), dressed in the attire of a Muslim sultan with red turban and saber, his beard cropped. The skin tones of both Prester John and the sultan are very similar – light – with the sultan having Eastern facial features compared to the priestÕs European features.

Figure 7

. A 1572 Spanish Portolano by Jaimes Olives, one of nine maps (Fig. 8), [15] shows Prester John west of the Nile, depicted as a Pope with mitered hat and scepter, blessing the crowd and facing West. His is the sole image on the map with a European likeness; all other represented rulers are depicted as indigenous or "exotic".

Figure 8

. Abraham OrteliusÕ map of the land of Prester John, published in Theatrum Orbis Terrarum (Fig. 9), [16] shows that as late as 1585 the legend of the Prester still spoke to European audiences. Here, there is no depiction of the ruler; however, the entire map is titled by his name, and his coat of arms adorns the top left. As the global historian Jon Carlson sums it up nicely, no matter where they placed him, any map depicting Africa during the 300 years after Marco Polo was not considered complete without a reference to Prester John. [17]

Figure 9

. The varied placement and movement of Prester John in this great variety of texts and maps reveals a kind of parallel phenomemon to that of Amerasia. Historians and mapmakers mixed and fused Ethiopia and India in the later Middle Ages much as they would intermingle Asia and America. The priest-king moved around between these two realms much as the Great Khan did in Amerasia.

We never actually ever see Prester John in Amerasia; however, we know that Columbus was purported to own a copy of the Letter of Prester John. [18] In 1493, shortly after Columbus had returned from his first voyage, the Florentine priest Giuliano Dati produced a cheaply printed leaflet that today survives in only a handful of original copies, his La lettera dellÕisole che ha trovato nuovamente il Re di Spagna (1493) in which he refashioned ColumbusÕs letter into ottava rima, to be sung on the streets of late fifteenth-century Florence so that everyday people could hear of the deeds of the Italian admiral. DatiÕs first song was so popular that he quickly composed two sequels, La gran magnificentia del Prete Ianni Signore dellÕIndia Maggiore dellÕEtiopia (1493-4), and El secondo cantare dellÕIndia (1494-5), both songs about Oriental India, one describing the power and magnificence of the mythic Indian monarch Prester John (adapted from the Prester John Letter), and the other the marvels of India (Figs. 10a, 10b). [19] For Dati, and others, including DatiÕs urban audiences, the lands to which Columbus had traveled geographically and culturally overlapped with the mythical eastern lands of Prester John.
