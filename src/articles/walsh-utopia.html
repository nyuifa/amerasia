Antiquity Beyond the Horizon: Thomas More's Utopia and the New World

Grace Walsh

On Vavassore's 1558 edition of the Caspar Vopel world map, just below the continental label of AMERICA NOVA ORBIS PARS, the mapmaker provides this observation: "Incolae Americae sua in libertate vivunt nullique obedient," ("The inhabitants of America live in their freedom, obeying no one"). The lifestyle of the people of the New World, first encountered by Europeans on the cusp of the sixteenth century, was a lightning rod for the European imagination. Seen as simultaneously idyllic and irrational, hedonistic and naïve, the intellectual and creative minds of the period grappled with the geographical, philosophical, social, and spiritual character of these people, through both word and image. Conceived some forty years before Vopel voiced his own such fascination on the cartographic plane, Sir Thomas More's apocryphal Utopia blended fact and fiction, satire and sermon, to create a New World account that coyly straddles the boundaries between plausible travel testimony, absurd moral fantasy, and political treatise.

The story, written as an audience between More, fellow real-life humanist Peter Giles and fictional character Raphael Hythlodaeus Organized into a two-book structure, Hythlodaeus first expounds on a critique of the inequity of European–predominantly English–society, and then offers a description of the island commonwealth of Utopia as a moralizing counterexample. According to his account, after staying behind with a contingent from Amerigo Vespucci's fourth voyage in the New World and traveling westward through diversely peopled lands, Hythlodaeus came upon Utopia, an island society with a sophisticated political, economic, and social system based around communitarian principles: property in common, a disdain for gold and precious stones, religious tolerance, pacifism; equal distribution of labor, and a strict penal system. First published in 1516, Utopia's New World setting has been the subject of scrutiny as centuries of Morean scholars have attempted to untangle its course among the many philosophical threads woven into this improbable tale.

While the references, both direct and indirect, to the details of Vespucci's letters (see Weitzman essay on Buti frescoes and Vespucci) are clear in the text, there has nonetheless been much debate about the extent to which accounts of transatlantic exploration shaped the overall content and motifs of Utopia. One argument is that More's vision of a peaceful, orderly commonwealth is far too estranged from Vespucci's description of naked, uncouth, and sometimes violent New World natives, and so this inspiration must have been incidental at best.[1] Many scholars note that a clear emphasis on Greek culture and philosophy points to ancient authors such as Plato, Homer, and Lucian as More's primary influences, rather than any contemporary writing.[2] More certainly strove to promote ancient philosophy as a guiding principle for scholarly thought and rhetoric, and his circle particularly privileged Greek writing and philosophy over Roman.[3] As a noted Greek and Latin scholar, More was better known for his translation of Lucian than the text of Utopia.[4] However, these superficial incompatibilities with the worldly concerns of explorers' accounts do not preclude the importance of the New World as both a physical and conceptual setting for the Utopians. Rather, More's text often dramatizes the ways in which accounts from the New World were fitted into existing paradigms of geography and world history derived from antique models. Utopia repeatedly reveals and develops these paradigms to exploit the shifting terrain of new geographical discoveries.

Initially published in Leuven in Latin, several editions of Utopia were printed in More's lifetime accompanied by a group of appendices that reinforced the false authenticity of Hythlodaeus's story. The text is introduced as a true account related in person to More in the company of Peter Giles. Their mutual friend Erasmus of Rotterdam helped gather letters from accomplished scholars from their circle to preface the book along with a map of the island (the original designer of which is unknown) and a poem by the supposed poet laureate of Utopia with a Utopian-to-Latin translation. In the second edition of 1518, this map was updated by Ambrosius Holbein, who also provided an illustration of Hythlodaeus in audience with More and Giles. The various letters that accompanied the text– from Giles to Hieronymus van Busleyden, from More to Giles; from Erasmus to printer Johann Froben, and later from Parisian scholar Gillaume Budé to Thomas Lupset in the 1518 edition– all refer to the content of the book as though it were fact, adding to the illusion of truth. The most compelling component of the ruse, however, is the text's references to the widely published letters of Amerigo Vespucci.

Utopia was written approximately eleven years after Vespucci's letter to Piero Soderini describing his four journeys was first published in Florence circa 1505-1506 under the title Lettera di Amerigo Vespucci delle isole nuovamente trovate in quatro suoi viaggi. The following year, Martin Waldseemüller published his historic Universalis Cosmographia map depicting an insular western Atlantic landmass titled "America" for the first time, in honor of Vespucci. A Latin translation of the Lettera was included in the map's accompanying treatise, the Cosmographiae Introductio, and several historians have made compelling arguments that it is this version of Vespucci's letter that More accessed.[5] The letter's denouement of the fourth voyage serves as the point of departure for Hythlodaeus's account, creating a deliberate air of factual continuity. As Vespucci reaches the conclusion of his letter to Piero Soderini, he states:

Having done all this, we decided to return to Portugal, which was on a course northeast by north; we left behind twenty-four men to remain in the fort, supplies for six months, twelve mortars and many other weapons; and we pacified all the people of that land, of whom no mention has been made for this voyage, but not because we did not see and have dealings with countless people there: for thirty of our men went forty leagues inland, where I myself saw so many things I must delay relating, saving them for my Four Journeys.[6]

This intriguingly open-ended description provided an aperture for More to insert his character's account into a record that was still under scrutiny.[7] In the opening pages of Utopia, Giles introduces Hythlodaeus to More, making direct reference to both Vespucci's exact words:

He went everywhere with [Amerigo Vespucci] throughout the last three of his four voyages— the ones that everyone is now reading about. Except, on the last voyage, he did not come home with him. He begged and even threatened Amerigo until he agreed to let hims stay behind as one of the twenty-four who were to be left garrisoning a fort at the furthest point to which their journey had taken them.[8]

Other continuities with Vespucci's letters became interpretive in their descriptions of the peoples and environs of newly discovered territories. In some attempts to reconcile discrepancy between More's highly civilized (by European standards) Utopians and Vespucci's descriptions of New World natives that range from the nnaïve to the monstrous, there is debate that the form of the Utopian commonwealth draws on Vespucci's choice of classicizing terms for what he viewed as an essentially primitive society. In particular, amidst stories of cannibalism, lascivious sexual practices, murder, and naïveté (some of which was illustrated in no uncertain terms in woodcuts by Johannes Grüninger that accompanied the 1509 German edition of the Lettera he nonetheless describes the new territories as a paradise and the people within them as "Epicurean." A particular passage of interest occurs in a fragmentary letter by Vespucci referred to as the "Ridolfi Fragment":

...those inhabitants prize neither gold nor other riches which we value and hold in great esteem, arguing that contrary to this we said we bought slaves from themÉ. for, as I said their life is rather more Epicurean than Stoic or Academic, because, as I state, they hold no private property, nor have they divisions into kingdoms or provinces: in conclusion, all that they have they hold in common...[9]

Later in his letter to Lorenzo di Pierfrancesco de'Medici he states again that "They live according to nature, and might be called Epicureans rather than Stoics."[10]

While this term is far gentler than some of the more barbaric characterizations of indigenous people, it neatly encapsulates a number of Vespucci's observations: that these people were pleasure-seeking, non-Christian, and living in a state of prelapsarian naïveté for which an idyllic antiquity provided a convenient model. Thomas More liberally expands this construct beginning with Utopians' philosophy of pleasure, their primary ethic of happiness, stating:

They discuss virtue and pleasure, but their main topic of debate, their primary concern, is the understanding of human happiness ...they hold that its happiness cannot be identified with pleasure, then at least pleasure is the main factor that makes for happiness...[11]

He further applies an Epicurean model in his description of their legal and penal systems. The harsh consequences for lawbreakers–slavery, exile or death– rely on a universal and idealistic ethic of civic obligation where social accountability and self-interest are intertwined.

Vespucci's letters may have left an opening for a Classical or Hellenistic imaginary of a New World society, but his explicit references to antiquity are limited. Nevertheless, More adopts and develops this model in a way that transcends analogy, devising a literal Mediterranean origin for the people of Utopia:

According to their history books, they had no information whatever regarding our part of the world (they call us the Ultra-equatorials) before I made landfall there, with one exception. Twelve-hundred years ago a ship, which had been blown off course by a storm, was wrecked on the island of Utopia. Some Romans and Egyptians were tossed onto the shore and remained on the island for the rest of their lives.[12]

A significant tool that More uses to forge this heritage is language and wordplay, noting the the Utopians' ease in developing proficiency in Greek in one passage [13] and later explaining that the Utopians must be descended from the Greeks because their language is etymologically similar. [14] This justifies the many Greek pun names for locations, demonyms, and characters that populate Utopia. Most significant among them is the name for Utopia itself, which elicits two simultaneous translations: Eutopia(ευτο¹ ος), meaning "good place", and Ou-topia (ουτο¹ ος), meaning "no place". Raphael Hythlodaeus' name is a humorous compound of hythlo and daeus (δα ιων, υθλος), meaning peddler or distributor of nonsense. These tongue-in-cheek epithets are found throughout, from the Utopian capital, Amaurot (αμα υρος, "Unknown" or "Dark" City), to a fictional society Hythlodaeus encountered in his travels through Persia called the Polylerites (¹ ολυ, ληρος, "people of much nonsense.")[15]

These names are couched in a dry humor that hints at the murky authenticity of the account, but they also reflect the geographical ambiguity of the New World in European consciousness. The veracity of accounts from New World journeys did not go undisputed (even Vespucci's letters), and the understanding of the New World's spatial relationship to both Europe and known Asian territories evolved and was adapted throughout the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries in response to changing information. Historian Toby Lester has suggested that More's use of wordplay, particularly the double meaning of "Utopia", may have been directly inspired by Waldseemüller’s Cosmographiae Introductio, where writer Matthias Ringmann's historic choice to christen the New World "America" may have employed the same logic of the "no-place" as clever subtext to its overt association with Amerigo Vespucci.[16] For Vespucci, the eastern edge of what is now called South America represented the confluence of paradise (link to DRACONIS entry), heathendom, and the far more mundane trading pathway to Malacca.[17] For More, these overlapping identities spoke to a mythological reality.

As part of his sleight-of-hand with the truth, Thomas More leaves the location of the island of Utopia to the imagination of the reader, stating in his preface letter to Peter Giles that he "forgot to ask ...just where in the New World Utopia is to be found."[18] Keeping its location secret allowed it to reside in a liminal state where the mythical and the real coexist. Nonetheless he localizes Utopia using a zonal model of the earth. Unlike Vespucci, who refers to passing through the "torrid" equatorial zone only to refute its supposed hostility[19], More refers implicitly to the zonal model and reinforces it through descriptions of the harsh equatorial landscape:

To be sure, south of the equator, and indeed on either side of it for almost as far as the sun is distant from the earth, there lie vast empty deserts, scorched by the perpetual heat. In every direction the view is gloomy and dismal. Nature is wild and uncultivated. There are savage beasts and dangerous serpents, and a few men who are no less savage and dangerous than the creatures they live among. But if you keep traveling, slowly nature becomes more friendly. The climate is less harsh, the sun less scorching, the creatures les predatory. At length you reach civilization again."[20]

Describing the journey through the torrid zone and beyond, More fills in the gaps between zonal geography and the Greek and New World imaginaries by describing the monsters found in the torrid zone in mythological terms: "Scyllas, or rapacious Celaenos, or man-eating Laestrygonians, or other monsters just as remarkable.[21] Expressed in recognizable terms, the man-eating giants and sea serpents of Odysseus and the Amazons of Heracles neatly enjoin with reports of Patagonian giants, Amazons, and cannibals that populate literature and cartography charting the exploration of the New World, including the Vopel map.

The Utopians themselves also endorse, albeit obliquely, a zonal construct of the world. Despite having "no information whatever regarding [Europe]," Hythlodaeus reports, "they call us the Ultra-equatorials."[22] More does not expand on Utopian notions of "Ultra-equatorial" geography, but paints an overall picture of a people with scientific and philosophical competence on par with the antique Mediterranean, particularly in "music, logic, arithmetic, and geometry," but astronomy above all else.[23] The zonal model being based originally on Pythagorean constructs and having evolved through a series of ancient adaptations concluding in Macrobius,[24] it is reasonable for the reader to believe that the Utopians have come to parallel conclusions about the antipodes as the Europeans, and the reference to "Ultra-equatorials" posits the equator as the boundary for that reflection. Beyond that frontier, the residents of one side become both mirror and myth to the other, occupying antipodal temperate zones divided by the wasteland.

Guillaume Budé's letter to Thomas Lupset prefacing the 1518 edition of Utopia suggests a specific place that superimposes the mythical on the real:

[Utopia is] surely one of the Fortunate Isles, which may lie close to the Elysian Fields- Hythloday, as More himself says, has not yet told us exactly where it is located... a community comfortable with its own customs and possessions, blessed with innocence and leading what we might call a heavenly life- Utopia is not in heaven, it is true, but it is certainly far superior to the muck that pervades the known world... Utopia, an island in the New World...[25]

This may be adapted from Vespucci, who no less than three times in his letters refers to the "Fortunate isles" as a key portal through which he passed toward the New World, though in his case he clarifies it as another name for the Canary Islands.[26] Budé's letter appears to refer, along with Elysium, to the lands of the virtuous dead of Greece, evoking the image of a classical paradise beyond the eastern horizon. Here too, as with Epicureanism, Vespucci's casual antique analogy becomes a defining model for the New World of Utopia.

Budé's mythological localization coincides with or perhaps prefigures the thinking of several key writers on the New World, specifically BartolomŽ de las Casas whose Historia de Las Indias (1527) hypothesizes that Plato's Atlantis was not a fable but a reality proven by the existence of the New World, a massive continent west of the mouth of the Strait of Gibraltar.[27] Gonzalo Fernández de Oviedo hypothesizes in his La Historia general de las Indias (1535) that the West Indies were actually the legendary Hesperides, island home of the demigoddess guardians of Atalanta's golden apples, which was also associated with the legendary Arcadia.[28] The common location of these mythological analogues– Elysium, Fortunata, Atlantis, the Hesperides– beyond the western horizon of the Mediterranean creates a plausibility born of geographic consensus and convergence with the New World discoveries. In maps of the 16th century, including the 1520 Johannes Schšner Globe, the 1540 Sebastian Münster map and Caspar Vopel's, the Hesperides can be found in varying positions in the Atlantic ocean. Vopel goes a step further in mythologizing the Atlantic by including a less heavenly destination– Gorgonum insulae, islands of the Gorgons– just west of Madeira.

This shifting horizon also subtly reorients More's vector of the known-to-unknown world from the zonal north/south axis to one wherein continental Europe no longer represents a clear western extreme and the border between a new west and the known, ancient east is undefined. In Vopel's map this is expressed through the visual geographic continuity between an American and Asian landmass. In his letters Vespucci states clearly that he has travelled to the "Indian regions"[29] in a quest for passage to Taprobane[30] or Malacca,[31] and despite his overwhelming Greek modeling for Utopia, More did not neglect this crucial motivation; an Asian geographical identity is emphatically present. We know that Utopia must be located within proximity to the Indies, because as More tells us in his initial impressions of Hythlodaeus, that at the end of his sojourn in Utopia, "By remarkable good luck he was on a ship that was driven by a storm to Ceylon, and from there he made his way to Calcutta."[32] While this detail suggests proximity to the Indian subcontinent, a further clue– in Greek of course– also points to a convergence of the Utopian New World and India. In the Utopian cipher facing the frontispiece of text, there is also a poem by the Utopian poet laureate Anemolius, which is transcribed and then translated into Latin at the bottom of the page. The word translated into Latin as "philosopher" is phonetically transcribed from Utopian (supposedly evolved from Greek) as "gymnosophaon," a classical term that commonly referred to philosophers of India.[33]

Lastly, there is a repeated motif of Persian proximity throughout Utopia. It begins with the Persia-adjacent land of the Polylerlites, which Hythlodaeus cites in his Book One, and repeats in the language, history, and culture of Utopia: though Utopian retains "traces of Greek", it "resembles Persian in other respects." In discussing the country's founder, Utopus, Hythlodaeus reveals that it was previously called " Abraxa,"[34] and he twice names "Mithra" as the common name for god in an otherwise religiously heterogenous society.[35] This explicit Persian proximity is subtly enhanced by the invocation of near eastern gnosticism through Abraxas and the esoteric spiritual traditions of Mithraism but it also proposes a society with a composite culture that blends features of the familiar and foreign. The Utopians occupy a physical space that is just over the horizons, known but not seen, where the newly discovered extremes of east and west converge onto a unified identity.

Inevitably, this multivalent antique/Asian/American imaginary does not comprise a one-way trajectory for which Utopia merely bears witness. In what may be the only contemporary artistic reference to Utopia beyond the illustrations of the first and second editions, a curious print indicates the conflicting characterizations of the New World using Utopia as a pretext. The panoramic scene depicts a bloody battle between clothed and nearly naked men beneath the towering branches of a pine forest. The nude men have neither exoticizing costume beyond their nakedness nor distinctly non-European morphology, and in a first state print of the woodcut in the collections of the Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York, the only clearly identifying information is the woodcutter's mark. In a second state exhibited in Leuven in 2016, however, a poem has been added beneath the image that identifies the nude men as hailing from Utopia, 'not far from Morian':

Ain Insel hai§t Utopion/ Die leyt nit ferr von Morian
Da g[e] schach ain sollichs schlagen/ Hundert tausent hort ich sagen
Doch ist es eben vil der Jar/ Das ich gelaub es sey nit war
Wann wie wolt ain nackend man/ Ain angelegten pawren b[e] stan...[36]

Perhaps the woodcutter is making an early reference to Morpion, a land later cited as existing a quarter league from the Rio de la Plata by André Thevet in his travel account Les singularitez de la France antarctique (The New Found World, or Antarctike). He states the Spanish encountered wild men nearby[37]; could these wild men be the woodcutter's Utopians? In any case, his connection to the milieu of Utopia is not arbitrary. Hans LŸtzelberger, identified by his monogram, was employed by More's Basel publisher Johann Froben , and worked with Hans Holbein the Younger, brother of More's 1518 illustrator Ambrosius Holbein in illustrating Reformation bibles and humanist tracts.[38] He may have been intimately familiar with the Utopian narrative and other contemporary travel accounts. The choice to depict More's sophisticated, peaceful Utopians as naked, warlike drunkards (a figure at the far left curiously holds a beer tankard) speaks to the text's involvement in an ongoing dialogue between reports of the New World and the paradigms projected onto it by the curious intellectual circles of Europe. Lützelberger’s Utopians seem far closer to the people described in Oviedo's Historia, who find that the " idolatr’as y errores" of the indigenous Americans echoed the paganism of antiquity in a "religion of the Devil."[39] The woodcutter's poem remains ambiguous, perhaps alluding to the questionable veracity of conflicting New World testimonies and their interpretations by skeptically dismissing eyewitnesses to his invented Utopian battle: "Hundert tausent hort ich sagen/ Doch ist es eben vil der Jar/Das ich gelaub es sey nit war" states (in rough translation) that though "a hundred thousand heard [the story] told/ it was many years ago/ I don't believe it happened."[40]

Michel Foucault tells us that "Utopias are sites with no real place. They are sites that have a general relation of direct or inverted analogy with the real space of Society. They present society itself in a perfected form, or else society turned upside down, but in any case these utopias are fundamentally unreal spaces." [41] This may be a serious mischaracterization of Thomas More's Utopia. Its legacy has outgrown its original context to become the namesake of a vast cross-disciplinary genre of visionary worlds that quite often exist outside of physical space as Foucault suggests: projections of desire that passively represent an impossible ideal. But Utopia problematizes place and space in an inherently different way. More's utopia is full of improbable ideals and radical notions like any other, some that are shockingly revolutionary even to a twentyfirst century reader. But the space it occupies, though unresolved, is real: the New World.

This New World is not truly new; it is merely the unseen reaches of the Old World that have long existed over the horizon, now beginning to come into view. While his primary intention may be to promote the virtues of a society modeled on Greek philosophy rather than to discern the identity of new lands or attempt an anthropological portrait, the setting is key to rendering its improbability believable, appropriating the New World's inherent geographical, topological, and cultural mutability, already rich in contradiction and fantastical detail. He utilizes this manifold imaginary to disguise the fictitiousness of Hythlodaeus's account. The physical distance of America and the temporal distance of antiquity become analogous to one another, just as the north/south axis of the known-to-unknown becomes interchangeable with the axis of east to west and the Elysian fields converge with India; Hythlodaeus himself becomes an analogue for Ulysses and Vespucci simultaneously. Rather than occupying a "fundamentally unreal space," Utopia occupies a real space that simply has yet to be defined in fixed terms and so can realistically be all things at once. Unlike the utopias of Foucault, this space is not a passive mirror. It has its own evolving identity that reflects back, changing and influencing those working to define it.
