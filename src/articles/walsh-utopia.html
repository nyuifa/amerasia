Antiquity Beyond the Horizon: Thomas More’s Utopia and the New World

Grace Walsh

Incolae Americae sua in libertate vivunt, nullique obediunt.
The inhabitants of America live in their freedom, obeying no one.

(Vopel map printed by Vavassore, 1558, near Brazil)

Thomas More’s Utopia occupies an enigmatic space in the history of literature and political thought. The story, written as an audience between More and fellow real-life humanist Peter Giles and fictional character Raphael Hythlodaeus, blends fact and fiction, satire and sermon, to create an account that coyly straddles the boundaries between plausible testimony, absurd fantasy, and moral manifesto. Organized into a two-book structure, Hythlodaeus first expounds on a critique of the inequity of European–predominantly English–society, and then offers a description of the island commonwealth of Utopia as a moralizing counterexample. According to his account, after staying behind with a contingent from Amerigo Vespucci’s fourth voyage in the New World and traveling westward through diversely peopled lands, Hythlodaeus came upon Utopia, an island society with a sophisticated political, economic, and social system based around communitarian principles: property in common, a disdain for gold and precious stones, religious tolerance, pacifism; equal distribution of labor, and a strict penal system. First published in 1516, Utopia’s New World setting has been the subject of scrutiny as centuries of Morean scholars have attempted to untangle its course among the many philosophical threads woven into this improbable tale.

While the references, both direct and indirect, to the details of Vespucci’s letters are clear in the text, there has nonetheless been much debate about the extent to which accounts of transatlantic exploration shaped the overall content and motifs of Utopia. One argument is that More’s vision of a peaceful, orderly commonwealth is far too estranged from Vespucci’s description of naked, uncouth, and sometimes violent New World natives, and so this inspiration must have been incidental at best.[1] Alternatively, many scholars note that a clear emphasis on Greek culture and philosophy points to ancient authors such as Plato, Homer, and Lucian as More’s primary influences, rather than any contemporary writing.[2] As a humanist, More certainly strove to promote ancient philosophy as a guiding principle for scholarly thought and rhetoric, and his circle particularly privileged Greek writing and philosophy over Roman.[3] More’s friend Desiderius Erasmus of Rotterdam was at the forefront of this push, publishing the first modern Greek translation of the New Testament in the same year as More’s Utopia,[4]and More himself was a noted Greek and Latin scholar, better known for his translation of Lucian than the text of Utopia.[5] However, these superficial incompatibilities with explorers’ accounts do not preclude the importance of the New World as both a physical and conceptual setting for the Utopians; rather, More’s text often dramatizes the ways in which accounts from the New World were fitted into existing paradigms of geography and world history built on antique models. Utopia repeatedly reveals and develops these paradigms while exploiting the shifting terrain of new geographical discoveries.

Initially published in Leuven in Latin, several editions of Utopia were printed in More’s lifetime accompanied by a group of appendices that reinforced the false authenticity of Hythlodaeus’s story. The text is introduced as a true account related in person to More in the company of Peter Giles. Their mutual friend Erasmus helped gather letters from accomplished scholars from their circle to preface the book along with a map of the island (the original designer of which is unknown) and a poem by the supposed poet laureate of Utopia with a Utopian-to-Latin translation. In the second edition of 1518, this map was updated by Ambrosius Holbein, who also provided an illustration of Hythlodaeus in audience with More and Giles. The various letters that accompanied the text– from Giles to Hieronymus van Busleyden, from More to Giles; from Erasmus to printer Johann Froben, and later from Parisian scholar Gillaume Budé to Thomas Lupset in the 1518 edition– all refer to the content of the book as though it were fact, adding to the illusion of truth. The most compelling component of the ruse, however, is the text’s relationship to the widely published letters of Amerigo Vespucci.

Utopia was written approximately eleven years after Vespucci’s letter to Piero Soderini describing his four journeys was first published in Florence circa 1505-1506 under the title Lettera di Amerigo Vespucci delle isole nuovamente trovate in quatro suoi viaggi. The following year, Martin Waldseemüller published his historic Universalis Cosmographia map depicting an insular western Atlantic landmass titled “America” for the first time, in honor of Vespucci. A Latin translation of the Lettera was included in the map’s accompanying treatise, the Cosmographiae Introductio, and several historians have made compelling arguments that it is this version of Vespucci’s letter that More accessed.[6] The letter’s denouement of the fourth voyage serves as the point of departure for Hythlodaeus’s account, creating a deliberate air of factual continuity. As Vespucci reaches the conclusion of his letter to Piero Soderini, he states,

“Having done all this, we decided to return to Portugal, which was on a course northeast by north; we left behind twenty-four men to remain in the fort, supplies for six months, twelve mortars and many other weapons; and we pacified all the people of that land, of whom no mention has been made for this voyage, but not because we did not see and have dealings with countless people there: for thirty of our men went forty leagues inland, where I myself saw so many things I must delay relating, saving them for my Four Journeys.”[7]

This intriguingly open-ended description provided an aperture for More to insert his character’s account into a record that was still under scrutiny.[8] In the opening pages of Utopia, Giles introduces Hythlodaeus to More, making direct reference to both Vespucci’s exact words:

“He went everywhere with [Amerigo Vespucci] throughout the last three of his four voyages- the ones that everyone is now reading about. Except, on the last voyage, he did not come home with him. He begged and even threatened Amerigo until he agreed to let hims stay behind as one of the twenty-four who were to be left garrisoning a fort at the furthest point to which their journey had taken them.”[9]

            Other continuities with Vespucci’s letters became interpretive, incorporating the peoples and environs of newly discovered territories into antique paradigms. In some attempts to reconcile discrepancy between More’s highly civilized (by European standards) Utopians and Vespucci’s descriptions of New World natives that range from the naive to the monstrous, there is debate that the premise of the Utopian commonwealth is based on Vespucci’s choice of classicizing terms for what he viewed as an essentially primitive society. In particular, amidst stories of cannibalism, lascivious sexual practices, murder, and naïveté (some of which was illustrated in no uncertain terms in woodcuts by Johannes Grüninger that accompanied the 1509 German edition of the Lettera) he repeatedly describes the new territories as a paradise and the people within them as “Epicurean.” A particular passage of interest occurs in a fragmentary letter by Vespucci referred to as the “Ridolfi Fragment”:

“…those inhabitants prize neither gold nor other riches which we value and hold in great esteem, arguing that contrary to this we said we bought slaves from them…. for, as I said their life is rather more Epicurean than Stoic or Academic, because, as I state, they hold no private property, nor have they divisions into kingdoms or provinces: in conclusion, all that they have they hold in common…”[10]

Later in his letter to Lorenzo di Pierfrancesco de’Medici he states again that “They live according to nature, and might be called Epicureans rather than Stoics.”[11]

While this term is far gentler than some of the more barbaric characterizations of indigenous people, it neatly encapsulates a number of Vespucci’s observations: that these people were pleasure-seeking, non-Christian, and living in a state of prelapsarian naïveté for which an idyllic antiquity provided a convenient model. Thomas More liberally expands this construct beginning with Utopians’ philosophy of pleasure, their primary ethic of happiness, stating,

“They discuss virtue and pleasure, but their main topic of debate, their primary concern, is the understanding of human happiness… they hold that its happiness cannot be identified with pleasure, then at least pleasure is the main factor that makes for happiness…”[12]

He further applies an Epicurean model in his description of their legal and penal systems. The harsh consequences for lawbreakers–slavery, exile or death– rely on a universal and idealistic ethic of civic obligation where social accountability and self-interest are intertwined.

Vespucci’s letters may have left an opening for a Classical or Hellenistic imaginary of a New World society, but his explicit references to antiquity are limited. Nevertheless, More adopts and develops this model in a way that transcends analogy, devising a literal Mediterranean origin for the people of Utopia:

“According to their history books, they had no information whatever regarding our part of the world (they call us the Ultra-equatorials) before I made landfall there, with one exception. Twelve hundred years ago a ship, which had been blown off course by a storm, was wrecked on the island of Utopia. Some Romans and Egyptians were tossed onto the shore and remained on the island for the rest of their lives.”[13]

A significant tool that More uses to forge this heritage is language and wordplay, noting the the Utopians’ ease in developing proficiency in Greek in one passage[14] and later explaining that the Utopians must be descended from the Greeks because their language is etymologically similar.[15] This justifies the many Greek pun names for locations, demonyms, and characters that populate Utopia. Most significant among them is the name for Utopia itself, which elicits two simultaneous translations: Eu-topia (ευτοπος), meaning “good place”, and Ou-topia (ουτοπος), meaning “no place”. Raphael Hythlodaeus’ name is a humorous compound of hythlo and daeus (δαιων, υθλος), meaning peddler or distributor of nonsense. These tongue-in-cheek epithets are found throughout, from the Utopian capital, Amaurot (αμαυρος, “Unknown” or “Dark” City), to a fictional society Hythlodaeus encountered in his travels through Persia called the Polylerites (πολυ, ληρος, “people of much nonsense.”)[16]

These names are couched in a dry humor that hints at the murky authenticity of the account, but they also reflect the geographical ambiguity of the New World in European consciousness. The veracity of accounts from New World journeys did not go undisputed (even Vespucci’s letters), and the understanding of the New World’s spatial relationship to both Europe and known Asian territories evolved and was adapted throughout the 16th and 17th centuries in response to changing information. Historian Toby Lester has suggested that More’s use of wordplay, particularly the double meaning of “Utopia”, may have been directly inspired by Waldseemüller’s Cosmographiae Introductio, where writer Matthias Ringmann’s historic choice to christen the New World “America” may have employed the same logic of the “no-place” as clever subtext to its overt association with Amerigo Vespucci.[17] For Vespucci, the eastern edge of what is now called South America represented the confluence of paradise, heathendom, and the far more mundane trading pathway to Malacca.[18] For More, these overlapping identities validated a mythological reality in new lands.

As part of his sleight-of-hand with the truth, Thomas More leaves the location of the island of Utopia to the imagination of the reader, stating in his preface letter to Peter Giles that he “forgot to ask… just where in the New World Utopia is to be found.”[19] Keeping its location secret allowed it to reside in a liminal state where the mythical and the real coexist. Although the exact location remains undetermined, he localizes Utopia using a zonal model of the earth. Unlike Vespucci, who refers to passing through the “torrid” equatorial zone only to refute its supposed hostility[20], More refers implicitly to the zonal model and reinforces it through descriptions of the harsh equatorial landscape:

“To be sure, south of the equator, and indeed on either side of it for almost as far as the sun is distant from the earth, there lie vast empty deserts, scorched by the perpetual heat. In every direction the view is gloomy and dismal. Nature is wild and uncultivated. There are savage beasts and dangerous serpents, and a few men who are no less savage and dangerous than the creatures they live among. But if you keep traveling, slowly nature becomes more friendly. The climate is less harsh, the sun less scorching, the creatures les predatory. At length you reach civilization again.”[21]

Describing Hythlodaeus’s journey through the torrid zone and beyond, More fills in the gaps between zonal geography and the Greek and New World imaginaries by describing the monsters found in the torrid zone in mythological terms: “Scyllas, or rapacious Celaenos, or man-eating Laestrygonians, or other monsters just as remarkable.”[22] Expressed in recognizable terms, the man-eating giants and sea serpents of Odysseus and the Amazons of Heracles neatly enjoin with reports of Patagonian giants, Amazons, and cannibals that populate literature and cartography charting the exploration of the New World well into the 16th century, including the Caspar Vopel map printed by Vavassore in 1558.

The Utopians themselves also endorse, albeit obliquely, a zonal construct of the world. Despite having “no information whatever regarding [Europe],” Hythlodaeus reports, “they call us the Ultra-equatorials.”[23] More does not expand on Utopian notions of “Ultra-equatorial” geography, but paints an overall picture of a people with scientific and philosophical competence on par with the antique Mediterranean, particularly in “music, logic, arithmetic, and geometry,” but astronomy above all else.[24] The zonal model being based originally on Pythagorean constructs and having evolved through a series of ancient adaptations concluding in Macrobius,[25] it is reasonable for the reader to believe that the Utopians have come to parallel conclusions about the antipodes as the Europeans, and the reference to “Ultra-equatorials” posits the equator as the boundary for that reflection. Beyond that frontier, the residents of one side become both mirror and myth to the other, occupying antipodal temperate zones divided by the wasteland.

Guillaume Budé’s letter to Thomas Lupset prefacing the 1518 edition of Utopia suggests a far more specific identity that explicitly superimposes the mythical on the real:

“[Utopia is] surely one of the Fortunate Isles, which may lie close to the Elysian Fields- Hythloday, as More himself says, has not yet told us exactly where it is located… a community comfortable with its own customs and possessions, blessed with innocence and leading what we might call a heavenly life- Utopia is not in heaven, it is true, but it is certainly far superior to the muck that pervades the known world…Utopia, an island in the New World…”[26]

This may be adapted from Vespucci, who no less than three times in his letters refers to the “Fortunate isles” as a key portal through which he passed toward the New World, though in his case he clarifies that it is another name for the very tangible Canary Islands.[27] Budé’s letter appears to refer, along with Elysium, to the lands of the virtuous dead of Greece, evoking the image of a classical paradise beyond the eastern horizon. Here too, as with Epicureanism, Vespucci’s casual antique analogy becomes a defining model for the New World of Utopia. Budé’s mythological localization coincides with or perhaps prefigures the thinking of several key writers on the New World, specifically Gonzalo Fernández de Oviedo, who in his La Historia general de las Indias (1535) hypothesizes that the West Indies were actually the legendary Hesperides, island home of the demigoddess guardians of Atalanta’s golden apples that was also associated with the legendary Arcadia.[28] Not ten years earlier, Bartolomé de las Casas Historia de Las Indias (1527) hypothesizes that Plato’s Atlantis was not a fable but a reality proven by the existence of the New World, a massive continent west of the mouth of the Strait of Gibraltar.[29] The common location of these mythological analogues- Elysium, Fortunata, the Hesperides, Atlantis- beyond the western horizon of the Mediterranean creates a plausibility born of geographic consensus and convergence with the New World discoveries. It also subtly reorients More’s vector of the known-to-unknown world from a north/south axis to a more modern axis moving from east to west wherein continental Europe no longer represents a clear western extreme. In maps of the 16th century, including the 1520 Johannes Schöner Globe, the 1540 Sebastian Münster map and Vopel’s, the Hesperides can be found in varying positions in the Atlantic ocean. Vopel goes a step further in mythologizing the Atlantic by including a less heavenly destination– Gorgonum insulae, islands of the Gorgons– just west of Madeira.

Vespucci states clearly that he has travelled to the “Indian regions”[30] in a quest for passage to Taprobane[31] or Malacca.[32] Despite his overwhelming Greek modeling, More did not neglect this crucial motivation and an Asian geographical identity is distinctly present in Utopia, even if thematically subordinate to antiquity. We know that Utopia must be located within proximity to the Indies, because as More tells us in his initial impressions of Hythlodaeus, that at the end of his sojourn in Utopia, “By remarkable good luck he was on a ship that was driven by a storm to Ceylon, and from there he made his way to Calcutta.”[33] While this detail suggests proximity to the Indian subcontinent, a further clue– in Greek of course– also points to a convergence of the Utopian New World and India. In the Utopian cipher facing the frontispiece of text, there is also a poem by the Utopian poet laureate Anemolius, which is transcribed and then translated into Latin at the bottom of the page. The word translated into Latin as “philosopher” is phonetically transcribed from Utopian (supposedly evolved from Greek) as “gymnosophaon,” a classical term that commonly referred to philosophers of India.[34] Lastly, there is a repeated motif of Persian proximity throughout Utopia. It begins with the Persia-adjacent land of the Polylerlites, which Hythlodaeus cites in his Book One, and repeats in the language, history, and culture of Utopia: though Utopian retains “traces of Greek”, it “resembles Persian in other respects.” In discussing the country’s founder, Utopus, Hythlodaeus reveals that it was previously called “Abraxa,”[35] and he twice names “Mithra” as the common name for god in an otherwise religiously heterogenous society.[36] Not only does Persia represent the Asian periphery of the ancient Mediterranean world, but the invocation of gnosticism through Abraxas and the esoteric spiritual traditions of Mithraism proposes a society with a similar origin to Latin West, but several steps removed in its expression. Just as gnosticism straddles a nebulous line between the Christian and the Pagan, the Utopians are both familiar and foreign, occupying a physical space that is just over the horizons, known but not seen, both east and west simultaneously.

The emphasis on Greek culture over any other antique identity speaks to a divide in humanistic circles that pitted the philosophy of Greece against that of Rome, seeing them as two fundamentally incompatible and opposing philosophies, rather than a concatenating progression in human thought.[37] In the midst of this culture war, the New World came to act as a mirror of whichever antique virtues or vices lent credence to a chosen intellectual or political polemic, and vice versa. In an anonymous 1541 tract entitled La Pazzia (“Folly”), which both in title and content echoed the anti-Ciceronian argument of Erasmus’s In Praise of Folly, the author– sometimes thought to be the translator of the first Italian vernacular edition of Utopia, Ortensio Landi[38]– appears to follow More’s lead in idealizing the Epicurean qualities of New World natives, stating romantically,

“How happy people would be without these so-called wise men [the “wisdom” of Ciceronian patria and gloria] can easily be judged from the life and customs of the newly-discovered peoples of the West-Indies…[They] are happy without laws, without letters, without so-called wise men; they don’t value gold or jewels; they don’t know avarice, or ambition… they are nourished by what the ground produces without any cultivation… as in the Republic of Plato, everything is in common… they live in perpetual love and grace, just as in that fortunate and truly golden age of Saturn in ancient times.”[39]

However, in the case of Oviedo's Historia, this innocence is stripped to expose the immorality of natives’ marital and sexual practices, cannibalism, and murder: “idolatrías y errores” that echo the paganism of antiquity in a “religion of the Devil.”[40] Oviedo’s objectives in drawing these parallels differ vastly from the anonymous author of La Pazzia and from More; he deploys a number of strategies for determining the origins of the New World that justify Spanish conquest. Oviedo uses the same qualities that Vespucci encapsulated as “Epicurean” to advocate for subjugation through evangelization.

Inevitably, this dual Ancient/New World imaginary does not comprise a one-way trajectory for which Utopia merely bears witness. In what may be the only contemporary artistic reference to Utopia beyond the illustrations of the first and second editions, a curious print indicates the conflicting characterizations of the New World using Utopia as a pretext. The panoramic scene depicts a bloody battle between clothed and nearly naked men beneath the towering branches of a pine forest. The nude men have neither exoticizing costume beyond their nakedness nor distinctly non-European morphology, and in a first state print of the woodcut in the collections of the Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York, the only clearly identifying information is the woodcutter’s mark. In a second state exhibited in Leuven in 2016, however, a poem has been added beneath the image that identifies the nude men as hailing from Utopia, ‘not far from Morian’:

Ain Insel haißt Utopion / Die leyt nit ferr von Morian
Dag[e]schach ain sollichs schlagen / Hundert tausent hort ich sagen
Doch ist es eben vil der Jar / Das ich gelaub es sey nit war
Wann wie wolt ain nackend man / Ain angelegten pawren b[e]stan…[41]

Perhaps the woodcutter is making an early reference to Morpion, a land later cited as existing a quarter league from the Rio de la Plata by André Thevet in his travel account Les singularitez de la France antarctique (The New Found World, or Antarctike). He states the Spanish encountered wild men nearby[42]; could these wild men be the woodcutter’s Utopians? In any case, his connection to the text of Utopia is not arbitrary. Hans Lützelberger, identified by his monogram, was employed by More’s Basel publisher Johann Froben, and worked with Hans Holbein the Younger, brother of More’s 1518 illustrator Ambrosius Holbein in illustrating Reformation bibles and humanist tracts.[43] He may have been intimately familiar with the Utopian narrative and other travel accounts through these associations. The choice to associate More’s sophisticated, peaceful Utopians with naked, warlike drunkards (a figure at the far left curiously holds a beer tankard) speaks to the text’s involvement in an ongoing dialogue between reports of the New World and the paradigms projected onto it by the curious intellectual circles of Europe. Where More projected the virtues of Platonic and Epicurean antiquity onto New World natives, here we see something more akin to Oviedo’s “idolotrías” projected back onto Utopia. But even this poem remains ambiguous, calling into question the veracity of eyewitnesses to the Utopian battle: “Hundert tausent hort ich sagen / Doch ist es eben vil der Jar / Das ich gelaub es sey nit war” states (in rough translation) that though “a hundred thousand heard [the story] told / it was many years ago / I don’t believe it happened.”[44]

Michel Foucault tells us that “Utopias are sites with no real place. They are sites that have a general relation of direct or inverted analogy with the real space of Society. They present society itself in a perfected form, or else society turned upside down, but in any case these utopias are fundamentally unreal spaces.”[45] This may be a serious mischaracterization of Thomas More’s Utopia. Its legacy has outgrown its original context to become the namesake of a vast cross-disciplinary genre of visionary worlds that quite often exist outside of physical space as Foucault suggests: projections of desire that passively represent an impossible ideal. But Utopia problematizes place and space in an inherently different way. More’s utopia is full of improbable ideals and radical notions like any other, some that are shockingly revolutionary even to a 21st century reader. But the space it occupies, though unresolved, is real: the New World. This New World is not truly new; it is merely the unseen reaches of the Old World that have long existed over the horizon, now beginning to come into view. While his primary intention may be to promote the virtues of a society modeled on Greek philosophy rather than to discern the identity of new lands or attempt an anthropological portrait, the setting is key to rendering its improbability believable, appropriating the New World’s inherent geographical, topological, and cultural mutability, already rich in contradiction and fantastical detail. He utilizes this manifold imaginary to disguise the fictitiousness of Hythlodaeus’s account. The physical distance of America and the temporal distance of antiquity become analogous to one another, just as the north/south axis of the known-to-unknown becomes interchangeable with the axis of east to west and the Elysian fields converge with India; Hythlodaeus himself becomes an analogue for Ulysses and Vespucci simultaneously. Rather than occupying a “fundamentally unreal space,” Utopia occupies a real space that simply has yet to be defined in fixed terms and so can realistically be all things at once. Unlike the utopias of Foucault, this space is not a passive mirror. It has its own evolving identity that reflects back, changing and influencing those working to define it.
