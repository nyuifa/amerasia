Iucatana

In the first half of the sixteenth century, as a result of medieval depictions of the seemingly infinite islands of Southeast Asia (link to Archipelagum entry), many Europeans understood the Yucatan, like California, to be an island in the Indies. In 1517, Francisco Hernández de Córdoba led an expedition to the Yucatan and named it the Isla Rica or Wealthy Island, and Juan de Grijalva similarly believed he had discovered a new island when he sailed along the Yucatan coasts in 1518.[1] Cortés’ map of the Gulf of Mexico that accompanied his 1520 second letter to Charles V (oriented with the south at the top) shows that Cortés shared this idea; underscoring this belief, the first Latin translation of this letter bore the inscription De rebus et insulis noviter repertis. Numerous maps in subsequent decades depicted the Yucatan similarly, including the Diogo Ribeiro map (1528), the Verrazano World Map (1529) and Giacomo Gastaldi’s highly influential Universale (1546). The Paris Gilt Globe (1528) and the Nuremberg Globe Gores (1535) both equated the “island” of Yucatan with Japan (link to Hispaniola entry). The cosmographer Joannes Myritius Melitensis described the Yucatan as an island as late as 1590.[2]
