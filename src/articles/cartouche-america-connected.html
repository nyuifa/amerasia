Cartouche: America connected to the eastern lands: Transcription and translation

Michael Agnew and Gabrielle Roehr

Narat [sic] Petrus Martir Asiam, Africam, Europam,/ & particulatim Indiam, Coluocanam, ac ad Septentrionem/ Suediam, Tartariam, Baccalaream, terramque Floridam/ omnes hasce regiones spaciosissimas, longisimoque [sic]/ dissitas interuallo continenti tractu & perpetuo si/mitu [simite?] cohęrere; atque ipsam pretera [sic] Americam ori/entalibus, & Coluocanæ annexam esse. Id quod &/ Almirantus ostendit, & uult  terram ad occide[n]temIn/dia recipi: Gangeride a[l]leua[nt?] a Septentrione uero/ in dextram ad glacialem oceanum ultra hyperbo/reos distendi: ita ut utrumque mare: Australe illud/ scilicet, & nostrum occeanum [sic]: in eius teluris [sic] angu/[lis?] [??]: non autem sepiant aquæ terram.

Peter Martyr reports that all these vast regions—Asia, Africa, Europe, and (severally) India, Acolhuacan, and, to the north, Sweden, Tartary, Baccalarea, and Florida—though separated by a very great distance that is continuous, prolonged, and uninterrupted, are at the same time contiguous; and, moreover, that America itself is connected to the eastern lands and to Acolhuacan. This is what the Admiral [Columbus] also shows, and he concludes that the land to the west is taken in by India (the Gangean lands rise up from the north), and that it extends to the right toward the Frozen Ocean beyond the Hyperboreans, in such a way that each sea (namely, that Southern Sea and our Ocean) within the corners of that land [illegible], but the waters do not surround the land.
