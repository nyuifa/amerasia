Cattigora

Vopel’s placement of the toponym Cattigara on the west coast of Peru shows Amerasian thinking at work. Cattigara was a received Ptolemaic name for a southeast Asian emporium that represented an important port along the sea route to China; it was the most southeasterly point in the known world for the ancients. The Greek geographer Marinus of Tyre (70-130 CE) described it as a port city supposedly reached by a 1st century Greek trader named Alexander. Both Alexander’s periplus and Marinus’ works have been lost, but Ptolemy cited them both as authorities on the geography of Southeast Asia in his Geography. He became interested in the precise location of this port, which received considerable attention in his text; he placed it along the eastern edge of the Magnus Sinus or what is today the Gulf of Thailand, on the Golden Chersonese or what is today the Malay Peninsula (in the very bottom right corner of his eleventh map of Asia). For Ptolemy, Cattigara was part of Sinae, or southern China, and it appears on the bottom right of Ptolemy’s eleventh map of Asia.[1] For all of these ancient geographers, Cattigara was of indeterminate location, and for this reason, was manipulated and moved into a great variety of locations by early modern mapmakers.

Ptolemy’s 11th Map of Asia, 15th century manuscript, The British Library, Harley MS 7182.

Tracing the movement of Ptolemy’s mythical emporium of Cattigara on early modern maps brings to light both the presence and resilience of Amerasia. Having read Ptolemy, a variety of sixteenth-century Europeans, including Magellan, sought out the mythical entrepôt of Cattigara, and cosmographers began to place it in South America on the coast of Peru, as did Vopel and his followers as a part of this tradition. The maps of Oronce Finé, the Paris Gilt Globe (1528), and Münster’s Novae insulae (1550) all show Cattigara in a similar position on the coast of Peru. The Venetian cosmographer and mapmaker Livio Sanuto continued to locate Cattigara in Peru in 1588.[2] The South American location of Cattigara reflects the pervasive belief that Southeast Asia and the New World were one and the same. Over the course of time, European maps moved Cattigara from its original position southeast Asia to Amerasia and South America. In his 1572 History of the Incas, the Spanish explorer and historian Pedro Sarimento de Gamboa placed Cattigara in the Pacific.[3]

[1] Modern scholars have argued that Cattigara either lay along the Red River delta near Hanoi or in Southern China. It has been identified with Funan, the ancient kingdom of southern Vietnam, or placed in the Mekong River delta, which flourished around the time that Ptolemy composed his Geography .
