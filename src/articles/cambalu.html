Cambalu

Cambalu, or Khanbalik (translated by Polo to mean "The Lord"s City"), was the capital of the Yuan Dynasty, the Mongol Empire founded by Kublai Khan (r. 1260-1294), located near the center of what is today modern Beijing. During the Mongol Empire, it was the capital city of Cathay, where the great Khan lived for at least part of the year in a magnificent palace that according to Marco Polo was the largest he had ever seen. "Inside, the walls of the halls and chambers are all covered with gold and silver and decorated with pictures of dragons and birds and horsemen and various breeds of beasts and scenes of battle." It was an emporium into which "more precious and costly wares are imported"than into any other city in the world," including treasures from India and all the provinces of Cathay[1] Sebastian Cabot"s contemporaneous world map (1544), engraved in copper and located in the Biblitheque Nationale in Paris, also shows the Great Khan prominently placed on the land bridge between America and Asia.

The Sebastian-Cabot World Map, 1544, Norman B. Leventhal Map Center, Boston Public Library.

As Vopel"s cartouches tell us (marked by blue pins on this site), many sixteenth-century observers believed the Great Khan (the Mongol emperor) and the Aztec emperor Montezuma to be one and the same person. The Venetian polyglot Tommaso Garzoni understood Khanbalik to be not far from the lands of Labrador and connected to the rest of the Americas. He described how leaving Tartaria, "you come to the land of the Mongols and Khanbalik, and passing into the Indies along this route, you come to the land north of the Kingdom of Tangut, and the Kingdom of Mangi is in the western part, the land of Labrador, and moving south, the land of Baccalaos, Nuremberg, Terra Francesca"Arcadia, Florida "Guatemala, Nicaragua, and many other provinces."[2]

Khanbalik"s Amerasian location hints at the Asian wealth to be found through the exploration of the Americas. In his 1493 Letter to Santangel, for instance, Christopher Columbus noted that La Navidad, the fort he ordered to be erected on Hispaniola, was well situated for the trade that would soon begin with the Great Khan on the Chinese mainland (como de aquella de all" del gran Can, donde havr" grand trato e ganacia. )[3] The association of Khanbalik and the Americas, sustained by the application of Polo"s narrative to Columbus"s voyages, made perfect sense; as the Italian literary scholar Leonardo Olschki put it, "Little distinguished Marco Polo from Columbus; both opened new commercial pathways aimed at reaching the Great Khan, both revealed new peoples for potential conversion, and at the same time, new routes to discovering the location of Paradise."[4]
