Quemquinafu on Vopel’s map is a Latinization of Quanzhou, the city Polo refers to as Zaiton/Zayton, the port in Eastern China where Polo discovered porcelain and then left to travel back to Europe by boat. Polo describes it as “one of the…ports in the world with the biggest flow of merchandise,” stirring sixteenth-century imaginings of the Asian wealth to be found in the exploration of the Americas.[1] Placing it about fifteen degrees north of Messigo, Vopel similarly tells us that “This kingdom was once wealthy and most powerful. It abounds in silk” (Hoc regnum fuit olim opulentum et/ maximum, abundat serico). On the Johannes Ruysch map, included in the 1507 Roman edition of Ptolemy’s Geography, as well as many other period maps, Zaiton appears at the end of a peninsula on the coast of China; on the Vopel map, however, although a port, Quemquinafu appears in the center of the Amerasian landmass, though a memory of the alternative toponym and the port function is retained: if we follow the river from Quemquinafu towards the coast, we arrive at Zoilon portus, the port of Zoilon. Vopel seems to have split Quemquinafu/Zaiton into an inland kingdom and a connected coastal port.

[1] Marco Polo, The Travels of Marco Polo, trans. Ronald Latham (New York: Penguin, 1958), 237. On Quanzhou, see James C.Y. Watt, The World of Khubilai Khan: Chinese Art in the Yuan Dynasty (New York and New Haven: The Metropolitan Museum of Art and Yale University Press, 2010), 158-178.
