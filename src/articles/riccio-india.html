India and indiano: The persistence of Amerasia in the Italian language

Vittoria Riccio

In the top left corner of his large-scale, printed, twelve-sheet map of the world, the German mapmaker Caspar Vopel inscribed this part of his map with the regional label of ÔINDIA ORIENTALISÕ. In tracing the history of ÔIndiaÕ and its derivatives in the Italian language, it will immediately appear clear that we are dealing with a fluid and multifaceted term that overcomes the boundaries of its most immediate meaning. The origin of the word has a geographical nature: it derives from the Greek Ἰνδία (India), which indicated the land of the Indus River (Sindhu in Sanskrit, Hindu in Old Persian); the people inhabiting those regions were called Ἰνδοί (Indoi). In its Latin form, India transmigrated into Italian vernaculars and was often used as an attribute for precious goods, such as minerals and pigments.[1] Since Antiquity, India represented an other place, a remote land inhabited by monsters and bizarre creatures in the minds of the Ancient Greeks, or the location of Terrestrial Paradise in the Middle Ages. These two extremes speak about a distance that is not only spatial, and that makes India a fantastic repository of supernatural otherness. The traditional ÒconfusionÓ between India and Ethiopia perfectly exemplifies this tendency.[2]

The discovery of the New World marks the beginning of a new life for ÔIndiaÕ; the sixteenth century represents, in fact, the apex of this longstanding philological disorder. It is well known how Christopher Columbus, certain he had landed in Marco PoloÕs Orient, became the first player in a linguistic misunderstanding that still persists today. A visual example can be observed in world maps and globes from the sixteenth century that concretely show the ubiquity of India; in Caspar VopelÕs World Map, for instance, we find several Indie, disseminated around Asia, America and Africa. It is important, however, to separate the historical ÒerrorÓ from the linguistic and cultural one; the former, resolved in a few decades with VespucciÕs famed intuition, was only the spark that gave life to the latter.

The five editions of the Vocabolario degli Accademici della Crusca are the perfect tool to investigate this evolution. The last publication describes India as follows:

From the well-known regions of Asia and America designated by this name, and rich of rare and precious products, India, and more often the plural Indies denotes, informally and figuratively, Great abundance of any thing, or any thing that is necessary, and sometimes even ÔGood fortuneÕ, ÔWealthÕ, ÔSource of wealthÕ and more; and it is used as ÔHaving India or the IndiesÕ, ÔBeing there India or IndiesÕ, ÔFinding the IndiesÕ [É] ¤ II. To come from the Indies means to come from remote places with customs and uses different from ours; and jokingly applied to those who do not know, or seem to ignore, the customs, or famous facts, of the place where they arrive, or where they are. [3]

This definition is permeated by a sense of flexibility that speaks to us about the multivalence of the term. India is not only a country, it is an entity (link to India Extra Gangem entry).[4] The geographical indicators only serve to blur its physical borders; Asia and America play an equal role in shaping its identity. India is a synonym for Wealth and Abundance and, at the same time, a remote land that is unquestionably different from us. The term becomes a container for any sort of thing, almost a shape-shifting body that adapts, from time to time, to our needs. We will see, however, how this apparently universal and fixed value is, indeed, more subtle, precise and variegated.

The entry for ÔindianoÕ provides interesting material as well:

Properly used as Ôof India or the IndiesÕ, ÔBelonging to the IndiesÕ; and said of a person ÔNative of the IndiesÕ; and informally in the manner of ÔFare lÕindianoÕ (ÔActing like an IndianÕ), which means pretending to ignore what one knows about acts, facts, or persons, practices, manipulations and more; almost to make it seem to be from the Indies, or another very distant place and therefore not informed at all of the things of the country. Same as ÔFare il nesciÕ or ÔFare lo gnorriÕ [É]. [5]

The idea of distance returns in this second definition, especially in the idiom Ôfare lÕindianoÕ or Ôto pretend to ignore something as if you were coming from such a remote land that you could not be informed of the facts of this countryÕ. The definition of the Vocabolario, however, oddly overlooks the consideration of the Indian diffused in the sixteenth century. The travel literature, the letters, and the accounts of the explorers all agree on defining the indigenous populations of the Americas as shy, timid, and na•ve,[6] an idea that easily translates into a negative conception of the indio, which becomes a synonym of uncultured, pagan, and savage. [7] ÔIndianoÕ and Ôfare lÕindianoÕ evoke the same idea of remoteness enclosed in the definition of India, but at the same time they imply a moral and cultural judgement. Curiously, the idiom stimulates a question that contains in itself the first germs of the Amerasian problem: which indiano are we referring to? The reference to the na•vetŽ inevitably points at the Amerindians, but the Ôfaraway landÕ corresponds to the Indies, East or West we cannot know.

One last observation on the Vocabolario regards chronology. The entries discussed above only appear in the fifth edition, published between 1843 and 1923, and they are still valid today. Therefore, we are discussing a relatively recent definition, and this raises a series of questions on the use of the term in the sixteenth century and the origin of the insightful description that we find in the nineteenth century. The only obvious mention of India, present since the first edition of 1612, is the term ÔindicoÕ. [8] This eloquent absence, however, is not translated into an equivalent silence: ÔIndiaÕ and ÔindianoÕ populate the vocabulary, infiltrating the definitions of many terms, from ÔgalloÕ to ÔpepeÕ, to ÔcaldoÕ and ÔtamarindiÕ. The occurrence of such attributes, moreover, multiplies from one publication to the other, to the extreme cases of ÔpoleggioÕ and ÔporcellinoÕ, in which reference to India appears only in the fourth edition (1729-38) and is supplemented by a rather longer definition. The use of India as an attribute is a clear allusion to the ideas of abundance and distance and it refers, at the same time, to the various historical conception related to it. The natural abundance of the Garden of Eden, for instance, or the tales of mythical reigns refulgent of gold and silver [9] could have explained, in the Western mind, the profusion of products flooding the European markets. (On the mapping of Paradise, see the entry on DRACONIS). It is not surprising, then, that the nomenclature ÔdÕIndiaÕ or Ôdelle IndieÕ was applied to plants, fruits, animals and luxury goods, especially if we consider the mercantile culture of the Italian peninsula, whose contacts with the East were mainly commercial. After 1492, the arrival of American goods in Europe encountered this sort of mentality, which resulted in an extension of the nomenclature to these new products. Whether West or East Indies, these goods were coming from distant countries and therefore their cost was higher; in this sense, ÔindianoÕ acquires a further semantic level, denoting more generally things with a non-European provenance, or Ôexotic.Õ

A perfect example of the various uses of ÔIndiaÕ and Õ indianoÕ is Giorgio Vasari. The first meaning we encounter is the simplest one:reminiscent of medieval writers and contemporary merchants, he uses the attribute ÔdÕIndiaÕ or ÔindianoÕ in reference to animals.[10] In other cases, Vasari indeed refers to the continent America, therefore using ÔIndieÕ with a precise geographical intent.

One example of this is the description of the Guardaroba in Palazzo Vecchio, where Ignazio Danti painted Ôthe Tables of Ptolemy, all measured with perfect accuracy and corrected after the most recent authorities, with exact charts of navigation and their scales for measuring and degrees, done with supreme diligence; and with these are all the names, both ancient and modernÕ.[11] The tables accurately described by Vasari depict the known world, which, he tells us, is divided in four equal parts: Europe, Africa, Asia and the West Indies.[12] The Indies are mentioned again in relation to the portrait of Amerigo Vespucci Ôwho made the voyages to the Indies,Õ [13] and one last time in the description of the Palazzo degli Spini, in Florence:

But to return to the space of the faade which, turning from the straight, was supported against the Palazzo degli Spini. In it was seen, painted in chiaroscuro, a Nymph all unadorned and little less than nude, placed between many new kinds of animals, who stood for the new land of Peru, with the other new West Indies, discovered and ruled for the most part under the auspices of the most fortunate House of Austria.[14]

The recognition of the existence of a fourth part of the world, equal and separated from the other continents, does not stop Vasari from using the word ÔindianoÕ in and ambiguous and fluid manner, sometimes as a mere signifier for ÔexoticÕ or ÔforeignÕ, sometimes charged with deeper meanings. The idea of distance is present in his description of the Adoration of the Magi (1496) by Filippino Lippi. The painting, commissioned by the friars of San Donato Scopeto, illustrates the Augustinian tradition, specifically the Homilies of Saint Augustine. According to the text, on that day Christ was offered to the adoration of pagans, alluding to his mission as the Savior of all the people of the world. The crowded scene is filled with Indians, Moors and bizarre costumes,[15] an expedient through which Lippi illustrates the Homilies and Vasari narrates them; turbans and camels, pearls and sandals in the painting have the same value that the words ÔindianiÕ and ÔMoriÕ have in the text. [For an essay on the Magi in the context of the New World discoveries, see the essay by Scarlett Strauss.]

A similar concept is at work in the Tribute to Caesar (1520 ca.) a fresco by Andrea Del Sarto, in the Medicean Villa of Poggio a Caiano.

In this picture Andrea, in order to surpass Franciabigio and Jacopo, subjected himself to unexampled labour, drawing in it a magnificent perspective-view and a very masterly flight of steps, which formed the ascent to the throne of C¾sar. And these steps he adorned with very well-designed statues, not being content with having proved the beauty of his genius in the variety of figures that are carrying on their backs all those different animals, such as the figure of an Indian who is wearing a yellow coat, and carrying on his shoulders a cage drawn in perspective with some parrots both within it and without, the whole being rarely beautiful; and such, also, as some who are leading Indian goats, lions, giraffes, panthers, lynxes, and apes, with Moors and other lovely things of fancy, all grouped in a beautiful manner and executed divinely well in fresco[16]

The most important element in this passage is the Ôfigura indianaÕ. The choice of these words poses a problem: why is Vasari referring to this figure as ÔIndianÕ? The man is seen from the back, his face is not visible; apparently there is nothing that would explain this unusual characterization. A closer observation, however, will offer some details that could clarify the problem. The figure is wearing pants, traditionally associated with an Oriental or Barbaric fashion (especially in an Ancient Roman setting); the mention of the yellow cloak could provide another explanation if we read it as a reference to the pigment giallo indiano. All these reasons, however, seem feeble, especially if we consider that the scene represents Caesar receiving a tribute from foreign ambassadors, [17] therefore justifying the presence of not one, but many, ÒIndians.Ó The text could help us understand: Vasari describes the figure focusing the attention on two elements: the yellow cloak and the cage with parrots that he is carrying. (On parrots, see the entry by Claire Lipsman.) The cage is filled with numerous birds, most likely coming from different parts of the globe, and is crowned by a magnificent, brightly colored specimen. Although not depicted from life, the disposition and color of the feathers and the form of the beak seem to suggest an identification with the Scarlet Macaw (Ara Macao), native to the Amazon region. The inclusion of an unmistakably American parrot reveals that Andrea Del Sarto deliberately chose to reference the New World in this work in order to show that people from every corner of the Earth, even from the most remote ones, came to pay tribute to Caesar, and metaphorically to the Medici. [18] The placement of the figure on the extreme left of the fresco, just as the Americas were ÒplacedÓ on the extreme ÒleftÓ of Europe, seems to confirm this hypothesis. It is important to note that Del SartoÕs work on painting the scene does not extend beyond the figure of Caesar; the right side of the fresco was completed in 1585 by Alessandro Allori. The later addition perhaps altered the balance of the original composition, enclosed between two very well-defined extremes: almost Columns of Hercules, the Ôfigura indianaÕ and Caesar serve as pillars; the New World on one side, open and unknown, and, on the other side, the embodiment of Rome, the classical center of the world, ideally replaced by Florence in the Renaissance. This symbolic system is perfectly understood by Vasari, who participates in the iconographical game by calling the figure ÔIndianÕ.

Other sixteenth century authors, such as Ariosto and Tasso, follow a similar trend. The Orlando Furioso and the Gerusalemme Liberata represent two more examples of the problem of awareness. In canto 15 of the Orlando, in a tribute to Charles V and his global empire, Ariosto elevates CortŽs to immortal glory and, in an epic parallel, Tasso uses his own canto 15 to honor Columbus as one of the most illustrious men of his time. The grand encomia paid to the protagonists of the age of exploration prove the interest of Italian intellectuals in the matters of the New World, but the disjunction of historical events and linguistic tendencies is evident in their use of the word Ôindiano.Õ TassoÕs narration, in particular, is set in an era which could have not been further from the problem of India, and yet he celebrates Columbus, while referring to Muslim generals and knights as ÔindianoÕ. The word here is a synonym of infidel, but in the great praise paid to the courage of the Ôfero indioÕ Adrasto it is free of any negative connotation. [19]

Along the same lines, sixteenth and seventeenth century inventories of Venetian, Bolognese and Florentine collections confirm the historical-philological incoherence and the persistence of the idea of Amerasia in the Italian language. In the inventories of the Medici collection, for instance, the terms ÔindianoÕ, ÔallÕindianaÕ, ÔdellÕIndiaÕ were alternatively used to denote objects from America, Asia, Africa, thus encompassing many things exotic or foreign. Mixtec masks ÔallÕindianaÕ, feather robes ÔdellÕIndiaÕ were displayed together with Asian artifacts in CosimoÕs Guardaroba, the same one described by Vasari as a representation of the whole world. The same happens in Bolognese and Venetian collections, from the case of Ulisse Aldovrandi and his indiscriminate use of Indian and American terms, to the mention of Ôa lizard, or another animal from America, the newly rediscovered part of the worldÕused in combination with ÔindianoÕ or Ôdelle IndieÕ in the Inventarioof Andrea Mantova Benavides (1695). [20] (On the Florentine Codex owned by Ferdinando I de Medici, see the entry by Louisa Raitt.)

To observe the occurrence of this phenomenon in visual arts, we can turn to Germany. Albrecht AltdorferÕs Christ Crowned with Thorns (1518), part of the Saint Sebastian Altar, is a parallel to VasariÕs textual fluidity in the realm of images. The background of the painting shows a secondary scene,[21] characterized by the odd presence of a figure that can be easily identified as an Amerindian, from the feather headdress to the partial nudity and the club resting on his shoulder. The presence of this Indian, at first rather bizarre, could be explained by the fact that the scene is set in the Holy Land, a place east of Europe, and it represents episodes from the Passion of Christ. A character native to the most remote place on Earth, possibly pagan and infidel, could help us put a distance between our Christian Europe and the barbarous actions perpetrated against Christ.

Altdorfer is also an interesting figure, since his case shares many similarities with VasariÕs. Both artists worked in an environment strongly fascinated with the New World: in Florence, the Medici were collecting American artefacts, just as was happening in Augsburg. The Florentine infatuation with the Americas developed during the course of the sixteenth century, taking the form of Ludovico ButiÕs frescoes in the Uffizi, or that of the Thornton expedition in the seventeenth century. (On the Buti frescoes, see the essay Margo Weitzman.) The Northern artists did not remain indifferent: the interest of artists such as DŸrer and Burkmair (part of the same circle of Altdorfer) is evident in their drawings and illustrations, and the Triumph of Maximilian I (1518) could be considered the final phase of the evolution of this interest. The People of Calicut, part of the procession in honor of the Emperor in the Triumphs, are the representation of this culture of exchanges, symbolizing at the same time the notions of extremities and fusion in a propagandistic and celebratory context. Expressly depicting Asian populations, [22] they are characterized by a mixture of feather headdresses, Brazilian clubs and elephants, becoming the perfect embodiment of this combination of Asia and America, historical awareness and linguistic neglect.

In conclusion, ÔindianoÕ and ÔIndiaÕ were extremely ambiguous terms in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries. They could acquire positive connotations or negative meanings, allude to distant and wealthy lands while hinting at familiar notions; they could refer to infidel savages and at the same time denote precious and luxurious product. The continuous use of these words in an undefined and malleable manner perfectly shows how, after more than five centuries, the mentality of Amerasia survives in our everyday language.
