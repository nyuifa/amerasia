India Extra Gangem

As Vittoria Riccio’s essay on the words India and Indiano makes clear (LINK TO RICCIO ESSAY), these terms have a long history of flexibility, both spatially and ethnically. Indeed, on the Vopel map, variants of the toponym India show up in Africa, eastern Asia, and on Vopel’s Amerasian continent. In effect, India was everywhere.

The development of the Amerasian idea relied to a large degree on India’s multi-partite geography. In this top right section of his map, for instance, Vopel indicates the regions of India alta, India maior, India intra gangem, and India extra gangem. For Hellenistic and Roman geographers, including Pliny, Strabo, Solinus, and Alexandrian astronomer and geographer Claudius Ptolemy, India was divided by the Ganges into two regions: India intra gangem, comprised of lands between the Indus and the Ganges, and India extra gangem, or India beyond the Ganges to the East—effectively, today, Southeast Asia--from the Ganges river to China (Sinnae), including modern Indochina, Indonesia, and countries on the maritime coast between India and China.[1] These lands were indicated on Ptolemy’s 11th map of Asia. According to the fifteenth-century Venetian traveler Niccolò Conti, “All India is divided into three parts: one, extending from Persia to the Indus; the second, comprising the district from the Indus to the Ganges; and the third, all that is beyond.”[2] Vopel added a fourth, Greater India of Ethiopia, India Maior Ethiopiae, in eastern Africa just north of the Equator.

Ptolemy’s 11th Map of Asia, 15th century manuscript, The British Library, Harley MS 7182.

The accounts of a variety of medieval writers, including 13th and 14th century merchants and missionaries such as those of Conti, Oderico da Pordenone, Marco Polo, and John Mandeville, all worked to develop a rich portrait of the entire Indian range.[3] In these depictions, India was a vast, populous, urbanized and civilized region filled with seemingly endless marvels, luxury goods, and extraordinary and monstrous peoples. It was famously visited by Thomas the Apostle (link to Thomas essay), who evangelized a portion of India shortly after the resurrection of Christ, was later inhabited, according to some accounts, by the legendary priest-king Prester John (link to Prester John essay) (link to GOCH entry) and his Christian kingdom, and was the physical location of the earthly paradise. India’s alterity and otherworldliness were inherently linked to its geographical extremity and liminality; it was a land that embodied both edge and origin.

India extra gangem represented lands generally beyond the frontier of ancient knowledge and about which geographers and travelers knew very little. In Conti’s opinion, this part of India “excels the others in riches, politeness, and magnificence,” and was home to sumptuous buildings, rich merchants, and inhabitants leading “a refined life, removed from all barbarity and coarseness.”[4] Pre-modern Europeans understood India beyond the Ganges as an ambiguous and flexible place; Ptolemy himself, for instance, left the exact location of the Golden Chersonese unclear. This allowed India beyond the Ganges to become a region where “travelers, geographers, cartographers, and audiences engage in an endless process of reinventing [it] in accordance with their own or their culture’s changing knowledge, needs, fears, and desires.”[5]

Most importantly where Vopel’s map is concerned, the remoteness and inaccessibility of these remote Indian lands allowed early modern geographers and cosmographers to understand India extra gangem as the location of the Americas; these both exotic and lesser-known lands could be adapted to encompass the Columbian voyages, which is why we see the toponym India Orientalis just west of Mexico on Vopel’s map. The concept of a multi-partite India functioned like a geographical accordion that could easily expand to incorporate newly discovered territories. It was for this reason that Columbus described the inhabitants of the lands where he landed in the western Atlantic the Indies (las Indias), and its inhabitants as Indians (Indios), language that would prove of enormous consequence in establishing an Amerasian understanding of where Columbus had gone. The numerous Latin versions of his Letter to Santángel published in the late fifteenth century, for instance, were almost all entitled "Letter of Columbus, on the Islands of India Beyond the Ganges Recently Discovered.” Richard Eden’s 1553 translation of the works of Columbus and Vespucci, the first printed English text to consider these voyages, was entitled A Treatyse of the Newe India. Indeed, generations of writers, including historians, cosmographers, and missionaries, continued to refer to the lands across the Atlantic as Indies, well into the eighteenth century. The historian Nancy van Deusen has demonstrated how “the use of the descriptor indio in Castile referred to people from the East and West Indies, China, the Moluccas, India, Brazil, Hispaniola, Mexico, and Peru.” The flexibility of the term achieved its maximum extension during the course of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries.[6]
