Iava Major

As our discussion of Archipelagus shows Java, an island in the Indonesian archipelago, was one of the many southeast Asian islands that formed part of the continuum in the European imagination between Asia and America. It is necessary to observe that Vopel has split Java into Java major and minor, further, he has split these two islands so that the eastern half of both islands is visible on the left edge of the map, and the western half of both islands is visible on the right edge of the map. Though it did not produce any spices per se, it had been an entrepôt for the global spice trade for centuries before Vopel made his map. We can see its Amerasian nature in a variety of examples. For instance, writing to his colleague Domenico Malipiero in 1501, the Venetian ambassador to Lisbon, Angelo Trevisan, stated that he believed newly discovered island of Jamaica was Java.[1] In the middle of the sixteenth century, the cosmographer Sebastian Münster visually underscored the continuity of the Asian archipelago with the islands of the Americas by employing the same print of cannibalism to illustrate daily life in both Java and the Caribbean in his Cosmographia (1550).[2]
