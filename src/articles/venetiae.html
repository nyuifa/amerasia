Venetiae

The city of Venice played a crucial role in the development of Amerasian visions of the world in two main ways. Firstly, it was the home of Marco Polo, the Venetian merchant who traveled to China between 1271-1295, and returned home to write about it in his Book of the Marvels of the World, also known as The Travels of Marco Polo (c.1300). Polo was hardly the first European to reach China; however, he was the first to record a detailed description of the Orient. His narrative account of China, India, Japan, and Southeast Asia inspired Columbus and generations travelers in his wake in the sixteenth century to look for and see Asia in the Americas.

Secondly, Venice was one of the greatest centers for European print culture in the first half of the sixteenth century. More than 150 printshops and bookstores crowded Venice’s city center between San Marco and the Rialto at this time, and the Venetian print revolution unfolded concurrently with the European Age of Encounters; these phenomena directly influenced each other in what became a dynamic relationship between print and discovery.[1] Venetian presses flooded the European market with books and maps about the New World, many of which, including the likes of Giovanni Battista Ramusio’s Navigazioni e viaggi (1550-59), as well as the maps of a great variety of Venetian cartographers, saw the world through Amerasian lenses.

[1] Elizabeth Horodowich, The Venetian Discovery of America: Geographic Imagination and Print Culture in the Age of Encounters (New York: Cambridge University Press, 2018).
