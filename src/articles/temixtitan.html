Temixtitan

Vopel tells us of Temixtitan, otherwise known as Tenochtitlan and later Mexico City, that “The kingdom and citizenry [were captured] by Hernan Cortes as a result of a battle in 1521” (Regnum et ciuitas/ a Ferdinandi Cortesio ex/ pugnata 1521). (link to entry MESSIGO) As the Latin American poet Bernardo de Balbuena put it in his poem Mexico’s Grandeur (Grandeza Mexicana) (1604), a detailed description of Mexico and the complex, opulent, and luxurious Mexico City as he knew it: “In thee Spain and China meet, Italy is linked with Japan, and now, finally, a world united in order and agreement.”[1] In the cartouche placed in the sea to the south of Temixtitan, Vopel explains that Temixtitan is in fact the legendary city of Quinsay (or Hangzhou), the Mongol capital described by Marco Polo, and identifies the Great Khan of China with the Aztec chief Montezuma. (Link to cartouche: Temixtitan)

[1] “En ti se junta España con la China/ Italia con Japón, y finalmente/un mundo entero en trato y disciplina./ En ti de los tesoros del Poniente/se goza le major; en ti la nata/ de cuanto entre su luz cría el Oriente,” Bernardo de Balbuena, Grandeza mexicana, Ch. 3, tercetos 38-39.
