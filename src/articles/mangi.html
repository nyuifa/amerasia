Mangi

Mangi, or Manzi, was the derogatory term used by the Mongols and Marco Polo alike to describe the barbarians of Southern China. The toponym is commonplace on Amerasian maps (link to Wu essay). On Vopel’s Map, Mexico is located in the Chinese kingdom of Cathay, and Chinese province of Mangi. According to the sixteenth-century Belgian cosmographer Franciscus Monachus, Mangi and Mexico were the same place, Mexico being the modern name for Mangi. Monachus argued that the medieval traveler Odoric of Pordenone made his way across mainland China and “came to the boundaries of Mansus [Mangi]. I think that presently the name is Messigo, as Ferdinand Cortes remembered it.”[1] (link to Messigo entry) The Venetian polyglot Tomaso Garzoni believed that from the province of Mangi, going east, one soon arrived next in Labrador, illuminating the persistence of the idea of these connected continents in the second half of the sixteenth century.[2]

[1] “Inde progressu viæ ad fines/ Mansi. Messigo modo nomen esse puto, eius meminit & Ferdinandus Cortesius, deinceps variis regionibus,” Franciscus Monachus, De orbis situ (Antwerp: 1527), n.p. On Mansus, or Mangi, referring to the Southern Song dynasty, see Mission of Friar William de Rubrick: His Journey to thhe Court of the Great Khan Mongke, trans. Peter Jackson (Indianapolis, IN: Hackett Publishing, 2009), 203, n. 2.

[2] Tomaso Garzoni, La piazza universale di tutte le professioni del mondo (Venice, Giovanni Battista Somascho, 1585), 358.
