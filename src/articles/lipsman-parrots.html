The Parrot’s Tale: the Bird’s Fall from Exotic Temptation to Common Commodity

Claire Lipsman

In Jan van Eyck’s 1436 Virgin and Child with Canon van der Paele , the child holds a either a Ring-Necked Parakeet or an Alexandrine Parakeet, both native to India.[1] Having been pets of Europe’s elite for centuries, it is not surprising to find either species here.[2] As a court painter, van Eyck would likely have had access to a live specimen to serve as a model for the parrot rather than having to paint from descriptions or borrowing from another artist’s work. Before this painting, parrots had not often been included in religious imagery.[3] To understand the parrot’s presence in this image and its increasing popularity after Columbus’s initial voyages to the lands we now know as the Americas, we need to examine the bird’s association with Paradise.

During the Middle Ages in Europe it was widely believed that Eden was in the East. The parrots Europe traded for were primarily Asian birds. In recognizing these two points, the connection between parrots and Paradise becomes apparent. They had been paired with other exotic birds, like their fellow Indian species, the peacock, in depictions of Paradise. This context makes the individual parakeet’s inclusion in van Eyck’s pre-Columbus painting all the more puzzling.[4] The painting does not depict a garden or an outdoor scene at all. Instead, the canon kneels in prayer with Saints Donation and George flanking the panel and the Virgin, Child, and parakeet enthroned as a trio in the center. The animal’s ability to speak gives us some explanation for its association with Eden and account for its presence in van Eyck’s panel.

The speaking bird was thought to say “Ave” in greeting, just as the archangel Gabriel had said to the Virgin Mary in the Annunciation. Dominican Franciscus di Retza wrote of the bird’s word in connection with Gabriel’s greeting, linking the parrot with the angel and the Annunciation.[5] In addition to voice, the impermeability of the parrot’s green feathers was associated with Mary’s virginity. The combination of these elements identify the bird as an “angelic messenger.”[6] Beyond the association with the Virgin, Carol Purtle explains that because the Christ Child is playing with the bird, the parakeet places the scene in heaven, rather than in an earthly paradise.[7] Instead of having it serve as a messenger to the Virgin, van Eyck intentionally placed the parrot in Christ’s hands, relating it to both the Child and His mother. Mary and Christ were often understood to be the new Adam and Eve, and the bird positioned between them may allude to this succession. By making the viewer recall the original residents of Paradise, the parrot’s presence works with the silk hung behind the group. The cloth’s rose pattern on a green background refers to enclosed gardens: Eden, metaphors for Mary’s virginity, and, in literature of the time, “a mystic setting for transcendent experience.”[8]

Purtle and John Ward have both proposed that the canon is having a vision of his postmortem introduction to Christ in heaven.[9] This interpretation clarifies the parrot’s participation in the scene. The panel was commissioned for van der Paele’s tomb as his health declined, meaning that it was created with a particular awareness of the canon’s mortality. Known for its greeting of “Ave” and its association with Paradise, the parrot turns to him and welcomes him to heaven.[10]

Van Eyck’s composition of the Virgin and Child with a green parakeet was imitated again and again over the course of the next century and a half. After Columbus’s voyages and the arrival of American parrots in Europe however, we see parrots appear in art more often. We also see a greater diversity in the species represented. Soon after Columbus’s voyages, we see ever more frequent depictions of macaws, a group of parrot subspecies native to the Americas. Over the course of the 16thcentury, Europeans became accustomed to these new parrots and began depicting them alongside African and Asian species. By the 17thcentury, parrots of all kinds were relatively common in Europe and appeared with some regularity in still lives as well as narrative scenes.[11]

Many visitors to the new lands across the Atlantic remarked upon the large flocks of parrots found there. Some took finding a species associated with India as a sign that they were in India, or somewhere like it. In October of 1492, Columbus wrote, “other than parrots, I have seen no beast of any kind on this island.”[12] He refers to parrots several more times in his journals describing first voyage. When Pedro Cabral landed in Brazil in 1500, he wrote that this “new land…[is]that of the parrots.”[13] As Europe familiarized itself with the Americas, parrots came to symbolize the mysterious lands across the Atlantic. Geographically located to the west of Europe, the Americas were often understood to be an extension of the East. Early on, the presence of parrots appeared to confirm this idea.

As the “New World”, a term used to refer to newly discovered lands in Asia as well as America, was charted through maps, parrots became emblematic of certain parts of the world. Parrots often appear on other maps as illustrations within the landmasses of Africa and what a modern viewer would understand to be South America, then thought to be part of Asia.[14] An early map depicting parrots in Asia is the Ebstorf Mappamundi from the 13thcentury.[15] The map centers on a heavenly Jerusalem and is oriented with East at the top of the map. A depiction of Christ’s head is in the East, marking the top of the map, and just below it, to the right of the two rivers that flow into Christ, is a parrot.[16]

The Cantino Planisphere , printed in 1502, is one of the first maps to depict the new lands discovered by Columbus.[17] It is also the first to use parrots to mark this land mass. [18] These new lands are shown as ambiguously shaped, making it clear that they are not yet fully explored. With no border or coast in the West, Cantino leaves the possibility the mass we now call South America may continue on into Asia. This idea is confirmed by the map’s original title, Carta de navigar per le Isole nouam tr[ovate]in le parte de India: dono Alberto Cantino al S. Duca Hercole or Map for navigating the new islands found in the parts of India: a gift of Alberto Cantino for S. Duke Hercole . Though he is depicting unexplored territory, Cantino follows Columbus’s example and assimilates it into Europe’s existing understanding of the world by describing it as part of India. The familiar green parrots of modern-day Senegal and African Grey Parrots of Central Africa are contrasted with the bright red macaws of America. The depiction of a species on more than one continent is atypical, even of later maps. Cartographers usually used animals to mark differences between places.

Here on the Vopel Map, Cabral’s “land of the parrots” is indicated twice. First, it appears in its Latin translation, “ Papagalli terra” on the landmass we now recognize as South America. The second appearance of parrots is in a fictional land placed in the same location as Antarctica. They are depicted in the eastern half of a land mass labeled as “ Antarcticus ” and “Terra Australis nondum cognita ,” which translates to “Southern land not yet known”. This phrase often included when maps depict Terra Australis. [19] The birds are represented by an illustration of three parrots accompanied by the phrase “ Psitacorum Terra” – the Latin translation of Cabral’s words.

The effort to distribute flora and fauna geographically was complicated by the absence of a clear taxonomic system. Although of different subspecies and modern taxonomic families, monkeys and parrots could be found in Africa, Asia, and South America.[20] The effort to represent traditionally understood distributions even as new species within these orders were encountered may have contributed to the understanding that the Americas were an extension of Asia. This interpretation of the new lands may account for why, after Columbus’s voyages, parrots appear less in places believed to be Asia in the East and more in places believed to be Asia in the West. The assembling of different species under the order Psitacciformes became representative of the “New World” as a whole, the place where East and West meet.

Because the Americas were originally considered by Europeans to be a continuation of Asia around the other side of the world, it was logical to place Eden, in the farthest East: in the Americas. The first known use of a parrot in a Temptation scene is Albrecht Dürer’s 1504 Adam and Eve engraving.[21] Erwin Panofsky’s analysis of the bird above Adam suggests that its presence is meant to offset the evil of the serpent behind Eve. This is consistent with pre-Columbian associations between parrots and the purity of virginity. The long tail on Dürer’s parrot indicates an American species. Dürer had three parrots in his household: his wife had a small green one, likely to be an Indian bird, and he received one from Málaga.[22] It is unclear if the third parrot was a second green one or a second from Málaga but it is reasonable to guess that it came as one in a pair. We do not know what species Dürer’s parrot was, but coming from a city on the coast of Spain, it is likely that it was a macaw from the Americas.

 Though parrots became more common in Europe through trade, they maintained their status as exotic and representative of the New World, that is, lands at the ends of the earth from Europe. Andrea del Sarto’s Triumph of Caesar employs multiple parrot species to depict this idea.[23] Two of the parrot types can be identified with confidence: the African Gray Parrots and Ring-Necked Parakeets inside the cage. The bird perched atop the cage may be a Red and Green Macaw from South America or a Crimson Shining Parrot from Fiji. Trade routes from the time make both birds possibilities.[24] While the body markings and beak size of del Sarto’s red bird have more in common with the Fijian parrot, the bird’s apparent overbite, where the top portion of the beak extends far over the bottom and white spot around the eye indicate that it is meant to be a Red and Green Macaw. With the understanding that this 16thcentury painting depicts Caesar receiving tribute from the farthest reaches of his empire, it would make more sense for the parrots to represent all directions in which the species was found, rather than just the lands immediately to the south and east of Europe.[25] Though the Roman Empire did not reach all the way to the Americas, del Sarto’s world had just expanded to encompass new lands as the Roman world expanded with each successful conquest. The inclusion of what we now know to be an American bird is also a reference to the newly extended reach of contemporary European empires.

 Prior to the exploration of the New World, parrots signaled that a scene took place in either Paradise or a heavenly realm. As the number of voyages increased between Europe and the Americas, a trade in exotic birds turned the parrot into a multivalent symbol. They represented the Americas and the vastness of the globe. They also came to acquire another meaning. Because parrots could be taught to imitate human speech, they were seen as a metaphor for the artist’s mimicry of life. Peter Paul Rubens played with these new meanings in addition to the bird’s traditionally religious meanings in his various depictions of parrots. His circa 1628 Adam and Eve is an almost direct copy of Titian’s Adam and Eve from about eighty years earlier.[26] The key difference between the two is that Rubens has added a macaw in the tree branch over Adam’s shoulder. The placement of Adam and the parrot on the same side of the painting as well as their spatial relationship, with the bird over his shoulder, are reminiscent of Dürer’s Adam and Eve engraving discussed above. Because it was now understood that Eden was not likely to be in the New World, parrots were not often used as a symbol of Eden.  They were, however, still a species that could place a scene in a faraway place. Because Rubens adds the macaw to an existing composition, he is amalgamating two artists’ works in one painting.[27] Irma Jaffe proposes that between the old belief that Paradise was in the Americas and the fact that Ruben’s access to macaws was a likely result of painting this work in Spain, the parrot may also refer to Spanish holdings in America. Rubens would have seen Titian’s original Adam and Eve in the Spanish Royal collections, where it resided at the time. The parrot is a nod to the traditional interpretations of parrots in art, a double reference to Titian and Dürer , and an allusion to the power and reach of the Spanish throne.

             The Adam and Eve was not the first time Rubens had included a parrot in a religious scene. His 1613-1615 work, The Holy Family with a Parrot includes a Blue and Yellow Macaw perched on a column base behind Christ.[28] The spatial relationship between Christ and the parrot is the same as that between Adam and his respective bird. Both macaws rest on a surface just over the shoulder of the figure below them. In Adam and Eve, the parrot passively watches the scene unfold.  In The Holy Family with a Parrot, it ignores the family and turns its attention to the vine grasped in its beak. This scene goes back to the association of Mary and Christ serving as the new Adam and Eve. Like van Eyck’s Virgin and Child with Canon van der Paele , the parrot makes the viewer think of Eden and the original sin from which the mother and Son serve as redemption.

Rubens’ depiction of Mary, Joseph, and Christ is one of the few examples of the Virgin and Child with a parrot that is not a copy or partial copy of van Eyck’s work. The fact that it comes 200 years after van Eyck’s painting shows that a connection between parrots and religious imagery remains in some form. The link in Rubens’s work may simply be that the association of parrots with Christian themes has become traditional through repetition. With too much diversity in its employment to constitute any one image type, the parrot becomes a motif of Adam and Eve and the newer, redemptive pair, Mary and Christ. A multifaceted symbol in Rubens’ The Holy Family with a Parrot, the bird works with the Oriental rug folded up directly below it to place the scene somewhere outside of Europe. 

As Europeans developed a concept of America as separate from Asia, the parrot came to symbolize the Americas in the west more than Asia in the east. This painting shows that the memory of parrots representing the East and the exotic survived into the 17th century and reminds us of Europe’s earlier association of America and Asia. By employing an American bird in an Eastern scene, Rubens depicts parrot species as interchangeable with one another across a range extending from America to Asia.[29]

Bruce Boehrer points out that Rubens paints parrots as both objects in a scene and as their own living beings. This is evident in the number of paintings that show different parrot species, including a portrait of a Scarlet Macaw from the 1630’s simply titled Parrot . [30] In this painting, the carefully rendered bird is the only living thing. Like many subjects of human portraits, it sits very still. A mountainous landscape stretches out behind it. At this point in history, the landscape may represent Europe instead of the parrot’s native territory.

By the 1520’s, parrots had become so popular in Spain that explorers no longer felt the need to include them in their listings of American species. When writing about the animals of the “New World” in 1526, Gonzálo Fernandez de Oviedo does not mention parrots at all. He glosses over their exclusion by explaining that it is a waste of time and writing space to discuss species that are already readily available in Spain.[31] There is archaeological evidence to support this widespread trade in exotic birds. Two parrot bones dated to the 17thcentury were found at Norwich’s Castle Mall, which was certainly not a hub of cosmopolitan activity at the time. [32] The number of macaw species represented by Rubens and the increasing representations of parrots through the 17thcentury are both indicators of the booming market for exotic birds.

The parrot trade developed hybrid species of macaws. In Ruben’s 1635 Rubens, His wife Helena Fourment , and Their Son Frans , we find another one of his macaws. This one, however, is not as easily identified as the ones examined thus far.[33] This bird appears to be a hybrid species, a phenomenon that is not known to occur in the wild. Today, the crossbreed is known as a Harlequin Macaw, resulting from a pairing of a Red and Green Macaw with a Blue and Gold Macaw.[34] The presence of this bird in a 17thcentury painting can mean one of two things. The first, is that a male and female of the species in question were simply kept in a cage together and happened to mate. The second possibility is that the hybrid was bred intentionally. It is impossible to know how exactly this particular bird came to be, but both situations would be the direct result of the parrot trade and the unnatural groupings in which the birds were kept. Surely once bird merchants were aware that macaws of different subspecies could breed with one another to create unique color patterns, they would begin to use this to increase their birds sales. Being a family portrait, the inclusion of a bird with out-of-the-ordinary parentage can be read as a reference to procreation. If the bird was bred intentionally, it may also be a reference to the careful selection of a spouse and subsequent courtship its human contemporaries practiced.

By examining parrots, we see how Europeans assimilated the new variety of birds into their existing notions of Paradise and the associated iconography. As they explored the region and better understood its geography, parrots came to symbolize the Americas and the expansion of Europe’s interactivity with the world. Finally, parrots came to gain their own meaning to Europeans, independent of their Amerasian origins. Boehrer notes how the increasing appearance of parrots in still lives through the 17thcentury demonstrates their fall in status. Prior to 1492, they had served as angelic messengers to men but by the 1600s they had become traded commodities.
