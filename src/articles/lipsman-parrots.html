The Parrot's Tale: the Journey from Angelic Messenger to Common Commodity

Claire Lipsman

On this map, Vopel indicates the South American continent with the label America Nova Orbis Pars. Below the word Nova, we can see the phrase Papagalli terra, or, "land of the parrots". This phrase was used in many descriptions of the landmass, most notably by Christopher Columbus and Pedro Cabral. Both explorers and the influence of their words on maps of Amerasia are discussed in more detail below. A more fitting place to begin understanding Vopel's reason for including parrots on this map would be to examine the symbolism of parrots before the Columbus's initial voyage to what became known as the New World.

In Jan van Eyck's 1436 Virgin and Child with Canon van der Paele, the child holds a either a Ring-Necked Parakeet or an Alexandrine Parakeet.

Fig. 1: Jan van Eyck, Virgin and Child with Canon van der Paele, 1434-36, oil on panel, 122x157cm, Groeningemuseum, Bruges.

Both small parrot varieties, native to India, had been pets of Europe's elite for centuries. It is therefore not surprising to find either species here.[1] As a court painter, van Eyck would likely have had access to a live specimen to serve as a model for the parakeet rather than having to paint from descriptions or borrowing from another artist's work. Before this painting, parrots had not often been included in religious imagery.[2] To understand the parrot's presence in this painting and its increasing popularity after Columbus's initial voyages to the lands we now know as the Americas, we need to examine the bird's association with Paradise.

The parrot's ability to speak gives us some explanation for its association with Eden and account for its presence in van Eyck's panel. During the Middle Ages in Europe it was widely believed that Eden was in the East. The parrots exchanged on European markets were primarily Asian birds. In recognizing these two points, the connection between parrots and Paradise becomes apparent. They had been paired with other exotic birds, like their fellow Indian species, the peacock, in past depictions of Paradise. This context makes the individual parakeet's inclusion in van Eyck's pre-Columbus painting all the more puzzling.[3] The painting does not depict a garden or an outdoor scene at all. Instead, the canon kneels in prayer with Saints Donation and George flanking the panel and the Virgin, Child, and parakeet are enthroned as a trio in the center.

The speaking bird was thought to say "Ave" in greeting, just as the archangel Gabriel said to the Virgin Mary in the Annunciation. Dominican Franciscus di Retza wrote of the bird's word in connection with Gabriel's greeting, linking the parrot with the angel and the Annunciation.[4] In addition to voice, the impermeability of the parrot's green feathers was associated with Mary's virginity. The combination of these elements identify the bird as an "angelic messenger."[5] Beyond the association with the Virgin, Carol Purtle explains that because the Christ Child is playing with the bird, the parakeet places the scene in heaven, rather than in an earthly paradise.[6] Instead of having it serve as a messenger to the Virgin, van Eyck intentionally placed the parrot in Christ's hands, relating it to both the Child and His mother. Mary and Christ were often understood to be the new Adam and Eve, and the bird thought to be from Paradise, positioned between, them may allude to this succession. By making the viewer recall the original residents of Paradise, the parrot's presence works with the tapestry hung behind the group. The cloth's rose pattern and green background refer to enclosed gardens: Eden, metaphors for Mary's virginity, and, in literature of the time, "a mystic setting for transcendent experience."[7]

Carol Purtle and John Ward have both argued that the canon is having a vision of his postmortem introduction to Christ in heaven, rather than the grouping of mother, Child, and saints being physically present in the church in which Canon van der Paele prays.[8] This interpretation clarifies the parrot's participation in the scene. The panel was commissioned for van der Paele's tomb as his health declined, meaning that it was created with a particular awareness of the his mortality. Known for its greeting of "Ave" and its association with Paradise, the parrot turns to him and welcomes him to heaven.[9]

Van Eyck's composition of the Virgin and Child with a green parakeet was imitated again and again over the course of the next century and a half.[10] After Columbus's voyages and the arrival of American parrots in Europe however, we begin to see parrots appear in art more often and in more diverse settings. We also see a greater diversity in the species represented. At this later point in time, art begins to trend towards depicting macaws, a group of parrot subspecies native to the Americas. Over the course of the sixteenth century, Europeans became accustomed to these new parrots and began depicting them alongside African and Asian species. By the seventeenth century, parrots of all kinds were relatively common in Europe and appeared with some regularity in still lives as well as narrative scenes.[11]

As Europe first began familiarizing itself with the new lands across the Atlantic, many explorers remarked upon the large flocks of parrots. Some took finding a species associated with India as a sign that they were in India, or at least somewhere like it. In October of 1492, Columbus wrote, "other than parrots, I have seen no beast of any kind on this island."[12] He refers to parrots several more times in the journals describing first voyage. In 1493, Peter Martyr wrote "the parrots brought from [the Caribbean] and many other things show that these islands taste of the soil of India, either because of their proximity or because of their natural properties," revealing how for Europeans, parrots connected the Americas to Asia.[13] When Pedro Cabral landed in Brazil in 1500, he wrote that this "new land...[is] that of the parrots."[14] As Europe familiarized itself with the Americas, parrots came to symbolize the mysterious lands across the Atlantic. Geographically located to the west of Europe, the Americas were often understood to be an extension of the East and from Europeans' first landings on these shores, the presence of parrots seemed to confirm this idea.

As the 'New World', a term used to refer to newly discovered lands in Asia as well as America, was charted through maps, parrots became emblematic of certain parts of the world.They often appear on other maps as illustrations within the landmasses of Africa and what a modern viewer would now understand to be South America, thought to be part of Asia.[15] Possibly the earliest map depicting parrots in Asia is the Ebstorf Mappamundi from the thirteenth century.[16]

Fig. 2: Artist Unknown, Ebstorf Mappamundi, ca. thirteenth century, paint on goatskin.

The map centers on a heavenly Jerusalem and is oriented with East at the top of the map. A depiction of Christ's head is in the East, marking the top of the map, and just below it, to the right of the two rivers that flow into Christ, is a parrot.[17]

The Cantino Planisphere, printed in 1502, is one of the first maps to depict the new lands visited by Columbus.[18]

Fig. 3: Cantino Planisphere, ca. 1502, Biblioteca Estense, Modena, Italy.

It is also the first to use parrots to mark this land mass.[19] These new lands are shown as ambiguously shaped, making it clear that they are not yet fully explored. With no border or coast in the West, Cantino leaves the possibility that the mass we now call South America may continue on into Asia. This idea is confirmed by the map's original title, Carta de navigar per le Isole nouam tr[ovate] in le parte de India: dono Alberto Cantino al S. Duca Hercole or Map for navigating the new islands found in part of India: a gift by Alberto Cantino for S. Duke Hercole. Though he is depicting unexplored territory, Cantino follows Columbus's example and assimilates it into Europe's existing understanding of the world by describing it as part of India. The familiar green parrots of modern-day Senegal and African Grey Parrots of Central Africa are contrasted with the bright red macaws of America.

Vopel indicates Cabral's "land of the parrots" twice. First, it appears in its Italian translation, "Papagalli terra" on the landmass we now recognize as South America. The second appearance of parrots is in a fictional land placed in the same location as Antarctica. They are depicted in the eastern half of a land mass labeled as "Antarcticus" and "Terra Australis nondum cognita," which translates to "southern land not yet known". The birds are represented by an illustration of three parrots accompanied by the phrase "Psitacorum Terra" – the Latin translation of Cabral's words. This phrase often included when maps depict Terra Australis.[20]

Europeans' efforts to understand new flora and fauna by distributing them geographically on maps was complicated by the absence of a clear taxonomic system. Although of different subspecies and modern taxonomic families, monkeys and parrots could be found in Africa, Asia, and South America.[21] The effort to represent traditionally understood distributions even as new species within these orders were encountered may have contributed to the understanding that the Americas were an extension of Asia. This interpretation of the new lands may account for why, after Columbus's voyages, parrots appear less in places believed to be Asia in the East and more in places believed to be Asia in the West. The assembling of different species under the order Psitacciformes became representative of the 'New World' as a whole, the place where East and West meet.

Because the Americas were originally considered by Europeans to be a continuation of Asia around the other side of the world, it was logical to place Eden, in the farthest East: in the Americas. The first known use of a parrot in a Temptation scene is Albrecht Dürer's 1504 Adam and Eveengraving.[22]

Fig. 4: Albrect Dürer, The Fall of Man (Adam and Eve), 1504. Engraving 25.2 x 19.4cm. Museum of Fine Arts Boston, 68.187.

Erwin Panofsky's analysis of the bird above Adam suggests that its presence is meant to offset the evil of the serpent behind Eve. This is consistent with pre-Columbian associations between parrots and the purity of virginity. The long tail on Dürer's parrot points to it being an American species. Dürer had at three parrots in his household: his wife had a small green one, likely to be an Indian bird, and he received one from Málaga.[23] It is unclear if the third parrot was a second green one or a second from Málaga but it is reasonable to guess that it came as one in a pair. We do not know what species Dürer's parrot was, but coming from a city on the coast of Spain, it is likely that it was a macaw from a Spanish territory in the Americas.

By the 1520s, parrots had become so popular in Spain that explorers no longer felt the need to include them in their listings of American species. When writing about the animals of the 'New World' in 1526, Gonzalo Fernandez de Oviedo does not mention parrots at all. He glosses over their exclusion by explaining that it is a waste of time and writing space to discuss species that are already readily available in Spain.[24] There is archaeological evidence to support this widespread trade in exotic birds. Two parrot bones dated to the seventeenth century were found at Norwich's Castle Mall, which was certainly not a hub of cosmopolitan activity at the time.[25] The number of macaw species represented by artists like Peter Paul Rubens and the increasing representations of parrots through the seventeenth century indicate the booming market for exotic birds.[26]

By examining parrots, we see how Europeans assimilated the new variety of birds into their existing notions of Paradise and the associated iconography. As they explored the region and better understood its geography, parrots came to symbolize the Americas and the expansion of Europe's interactivity with the world. Parrots came to gain their own meaning to Europeans, independent of their Amerasian origins. Bruce Boehrer notes how the increasing appearance of parrots in still lives through the seventeenth century demonstrates their change in status. Prior to 1492, they had served as angelic messengers to men. In the sixteenth century, they were emblems of Amerasia. By the seventeenth century, they had been demoted to locally available commodities.
