(Afro-)Amerasian Hieroglyphics? The Reception and Interpretation of Mesoamerican Manuscripts in Europe

Michael Agnew

Modern viewers of the map designed by Caspar Vopel and published by Giovanni Andrea Vavassore in 1558, even when aware of contemporary representations of Asia and America as a single landmass, are likely to be surprised by the close contiguity of East Asian place names alongside Mesoamerican ones. In the region of the map labelled Hispania Nova (New Spain), we find Temixtitan (Tenochtitlan, Mexico City) due east of Mangi (Manzi, or Southern China) and southeast of Chatay (Cathay, or Northern China), with a Messigo (Mexico) to the west of Mangi and a smattering of cities ending in fu (a Chinese administrative division signifying "prefecture") in the same general region as identifiably Mesoamerican place names like Aculuacan (Acolhuacan), all within the lands of Asia Magna (Greater Asia) and bordered on the east by what is now known as the Gulf of Mexico but that here is labelled Mare Cathayum (Sea of Cathay). Vopel's assertive amalgamation of newly discovered territories with East Asian geography promotes the idea that Chinese lands fell well within the territorial claims of the Spanish Crown, and not Portugal's. His explanatory cartouches that identify Moctezuma, the ruler of Tenochtitlan, with the Great Khan, reinforce this notion.[1]

Regardless of Vopel's geopolitical thesis, it is worth considering whether his contemporaries would find this imbrication of China and Mexico as jarring as it is to us. How could these disparate regions and cultures be so closely drawn together in his geographic imaginary that they effectively overlapped? The European reception and understanding of one aspect of the pre-Columbian cultures of the Valley of Mexico, their books and pictographic writing, can help shed light on this apparent quandary.

Alexander Nagel has drawn attention to a volume now in the Biblioteca Universitaria di Bologna that once belonged to Ferdinando Cospi, a seventeenth-century collector who eventually donated his cabinet of curiosities to the Bolognese senate in 1660.[2] As Nagel notes, the preliminary inventory of Cospi's collection, prepared in 1667 by Lorenzo Legati, describes the manuscript as a "book from China with various hieroglyphs," corresponding exactly to what originally appeared on the cover of the (European) vellum binding, which at the time read "Libro della China" [Fig. 1].[3]

Fig. 1. Vellum cover of the Codex Cospi, Biblioteca Universitaria di Bologna MS 4039, with "Libro della China" partially erased and "Libro del Messico" written over it. (Photo: Alexander Nagel)

The first part of the original inscription was imperfectly erased and corrected to read, "Book from Mexico given by the lord Count Valerio Zani to the lord Marquis Cospi on December 26, 1665." Underneath the palimpsest, however, one can still vaguely make out the original "Libro della China" [Fig. 1].[4] When Legati prepared a detailed descriptive catalogue of the Cospi collection a decade later in 1677, he rectified his characterization of the book, listing it as a "Libro messicano."[5] Did this shift (on the book's cover and in the catalogue) reflect some recognition that Legati's original description was erroneous? Or could "Mexico" and "China" somehow have been entirely compatible terms, and this change was simply an effort to describe it with greater precision, as if "Mexico" were a part of a larger territory called "China" or perhaps adjacent to it in an "Asia Magna"? It is difficult to say which. Legati is careful to describe other books and documents from China in the collection as "Chinese."[6] Presumably he could have known from contemporary maps more accurate than Vopel's that Asia and America were in fact not connected. Yet despite the watery divide of the Pacific and the scrupulous cataloguer's plausible desire for precision, it is nevertheless still possible that he and others who consulted the Codex Cospi did not conceive of that division as impeding some fundamental cultural connection between (our) East Asia and Mesoamerica.

In the second chapter of his account of language and knowledge in Europe before the Age of Reason in The Order of Things, Michel Foucault describes how the principle of resemblance subtended the interpretation of texts and the world in their interpenetrating relationship. Specifically, he defines four kinds of similitude: convenientia, a coming together expressing the adjacency of things in the great chain of being; aemulatio, or similarity between things that are not contiguous on that chain; analogia, a kind of superimposition of convenientia and aemulatio, which refers to analogous relationships between sets of things; and, finally, sympathia, which (for example) governs the movement of the four elements or the physiological humors and involves a transformative displacement of qualities between disparate things in the natural world (humors, elements, planets) that are connected at a distance because of their supposed affinities.[7]

All of these categories involve, in one form or another, an epistemological process of assimilation: ascribing similar qualities to otherwise different things. Strikingly, though his sources postdate the Transatlantic encounter between Europe and the Americas, Foucault does not explore the implications of these historical events, which surely could have tested European modes of thought founded on the principle of resemblances and provided a basis for the epistemic shift towards a taxonomic order based on the principle of difference that he situates in the Age of Reason. Foucault misses an opportunity to examine the intellectual ramifications of European exploration and colonization in Africa, Asia and the Americas, but his omission is perhaps not entirely surprising, given the concerted efforts European thinkers made during the two centuries after Columbus's voyages to assimilate a "New World" into the preexisting hermeneutic schemas that had developed as part of the complex architecture of late medieval and early modern epistemologies.

One such instance of assimilation, tied to the domain of books and writing—which, as Foucault points out, was privileged over orality in early modern Europe—Is European interpretation of the pictographic writing and illustrations in the few surviving examples of pre-Columbian manuscripts and in post-conquest codices prepared under the watchful eye of missionaries.[8] Their reception in Europe reflects the primacy of the written word and encouraged the incorporation of indigenous American writing systems within a larger narrative of the Biblical "confusion of tongues" in the episode of the Tower of Babel, with written words understood to be the underlying form of language, and all writing, however far removed from its origins before Babel, darkly mirroring the words in the "book of Creation." European attempts to understand Mesoamerican manuscripts can be tied to two intellectual currents, both of which emerged out of traditions of esotericism: on the one hand, the images found in emblem books, and, on the other, inquiries into the history of human languages. Both currents informed European scholars' interest in Egyptian hieroglyphs and other written manifestations of ancient languages; both are tied to a fundamentally visual or scriptural conception of language as a mysterious chain of resemblances linking words to things; both depend on allegorical modes of thought according to which a single image can encompass a complex statement.

Europeans who scrutinized Mesoamerican manuscripts understood their images and pictographs to be analogous to Egyptian hieroglyphs, a comparison that Pietro Martire d'Anghiera makes in his fourth Decade (first published in 1521) when discussing Mesoamerican books: "The characters are very different from ours, laid out line-by-line as we do, with small blocks, hooks, loops, lines, stars, and other shapes. They almost resemble Egyptian letter-forms."[9] This was not an unreasonable supposition. Though Pietro Martire does not use the precise term hieroglyphs, what exactly that concept encompassed in the early modern period deserves some unpacking, especially because Legati seems to have interpreted not just the pictographs proper in the Codex Cospi as hieroglyphs but also what are in fact illustrations depicting deities and rulers. The word emerged in the context of the discovery in 1419 of a treatise from late antiquity on Egyptian symbolic writing, the Hieroglyphica attributed to a certain Horapollo (a Hellenized compound of Horus Apollo), which circulated among Italian humanists. Early modern fascination with Egyptian monumental inscriptions was further encouraged by the contemporary excavation in Rome of sculptures and obelisks bearing hieroglyphics.[10]

Although Horapollo's Hieroglyphica was no Rosetta Stone, and Europeans remained fundamentally in the dark regarding Egyptian writing until 1799, speculation about the meaning of Egyptian inscriptions prompted Europeans themselves to create inventive "hieroglyphs" of the sort that appear in Francesco Colonna's Hypnerotomachia Poliphili (Venice, 1499). Most famous among them perhaps is the emblem that its printer, Aldus Manutius, later adopted as his own device and motto, the dolphin-entwined anchor signifying festina tarde (or lente, "hasten slowly"), paired in the illustration in Colonna's book with another emblem: a helmet, an ox-skull, and an oil lamp representing the idea that Patience is the ornament, guardian and protector of life [Fig. 2].

Fig. 2. Francesco Colonna, Poliphili Hypnerotomachia, facsimile ed. (London: Metheun, 1904), fol. D7r. Original from University of Michigan.

A rough genealogy may be drawn from Colonna through Andrea Alciato's moralizing interpretations of emblems in his Emblematum liber (Augsburg, 1531), through Pierio Valeriano's voluminous exegesis of allegorical figures based on commentaries on Horapollo, Hieroglyphica sive de sacris Aegyptiorum literis commentarii (Basel, 1556), to the most emblematic of emblem books, Cesare Ripa's Iconologia, published initially without illustrations in Rome in 1593 and soon reissued in 1603 with accompanying woodcuts whose number continued to grow in two subsequent re-editions before the author's death in 1622 [figs. 3-5].

Fig. 3. "Concordia," Andrea Alciato, Emblematum liber (Augsburg: Heinrich Steyner, 1531), fol. A4r. Original from Glasgow University Library.

Fig. 4. Liber XXXI, Pierio Valeriano, Hieroglyphica (Basel: [Michael Isengrin], 1556), fol. 219v. Original from The Getty Research Institute.

Fig. 5. Cesare Ripa, Iconologia (Rome: Appresso Lepido Facij, 1603), title page. Original from Duke University Libraries.

Each of these books present complex images that today could never be confused with hieroglyphs, though the title of Valeriano's work makes it clear that the term could be plausibly applied to such images in the sixteenth century. Each emblem, furthermore, is accompanied by elaborate exegeses suggesting that, in accordance with the contemporary understanding of Egyptian hieroglyphs, the images could function as shorthand for entire complexes of ideas.

In this light, Lorenzo Legati's attempt to understand the images and pictographs in the Codex Cospi—if not their semantic content, at least their function—becomes clearer. His description of the manuscript, following an account of its size and accordion-book format ("it is not divided into leaves, like our books"), indicates that the book contains "only HIEROGLYPHS from MEXICO"—Legati's uppercase—"which are very extravagant figures that for the most part portray strangely monstrous humans and animals" [Fig. 6].[11]

Fig. 6. Lorenzo Legati, Museo Cospiano (Bologna: Giacomo Monti, 1677), p. 191. Original from The Getty Research Institute.

These he divides into various categories, by size: "massimi," "mediocri," "menori," and "minimi." The penultimate class, "lesser" figures, are arranged in "marginal compartments, as if they were (and perhaps they are) marginal notes or commentary on the larger hieroglyphs," and the last group, the smallest, "likewise seem to explain the largest hieroglyphs, which they surround like a crown."[12] Though Legati identifies no analogous format for the manuscript (like an Asian accordion book), he does intuit an analogy for the small pictographs around the margins ("marginal notes or commentary") and situates the pictographs and illustrations in the general category of hieroglyphs, presumably basing his assessment on an Egyptian model—or, rather, on a model that encompassed also the elaborate allegories in emblem books, conflating those figures with Egyptian hieroglyphs as they were understood by European savants. The examples he provides include an image of the Lord of the House of Dawn, Tlahuizcalpantecuhtli (the planet Venus), and a throne, both geroglifici massimi in Legati's classification. The woodcut reproduction in Legati's catalogue, however, misrepresents what in the original manuscript is a narrative scene in which a fearsome god attacks the throne with his spear. In the catalogue, the figure has been reversed and does not interact with the throne, suggesting that Legati interpreted each element as a separate "hieroglyph" [Fig. 7; cf. Fig. 8] .

Fig. 7. Lorenzo Legati, Museo Cospiano, p. 192. Original from The Getty Research Institute.

Fig. 8. Codex Cospi, panels 8–10. From facsimile accompanying Ferdinand Anders et al., Calendario de pron—sticos y ofrendas: Libro explicativo del llamado C—dice Cospi (Mexico City: Fondo de Cultura Econ—mica, 1994).

Legati's description of the libro messicano appears in the second chapter of the third book of his full catalogue of the Cospi collection, under the rubric of "Books, volumes, documents, and esoteric writings" ("Libri, volume, carte, e scritture esotiche"). This section also includes various books and ink drawings from China, which immediately precede the erstwhile "Chinese" book, now catalogued as "Mexican." Legati refers to Chinese characters as such (caratteri) and not as hieroglyphs. Nevertheless, he cites at length the humanist and historiographer Cosimo Bartoli (1503–1572), who, after explaining that each character represents a word and that they would be better ddescribed as ciphers (cifere), explicitly compares Chinese characters to Egyptian writing: they are "ingenious work, perhaps even more witty than Egyptian hieroglyphs."[13] Legati notes that the Chinese write with a brush, something he also draws our attention to in the description of the Mexican book, which contains "works not of the pen but of the brush."[14]

We lack even an approximate date for the creation of the Codex Cospi, but the style of the images and pictographs as well as the book's folded format reflect a decidedly pre-Columbian tradition, even if it was in fact prepared during the early years of the conquest of Mexico.[15] Following the conquest, missionaries working in the Spanish colonies oversaw the production of newfangled books by indigenous informants, scribes, and painters: European-style codices proper, with hinges at the spine, whose imagery reflected the influence of European draftsmanship while conserving fundamental aspects of Mesoamerican pictographic conventions. (link to Raitt essay on the Florentine Codex). One such document is the so-called Codex Mendoza, perhaps commissioned by the first viceroy of New Spain, Antonio de Mendoza and probably prepared during his administration (1535–1550). The book presents a pictorial history of the founding of Tenochtitlan and the reign of its first ruler, followed by an accounting of the tributes paid to the Mexica rulers of that city by conquered territories [Fig. 9].

Fig. 9. Codex Mendoza, fol. 2r. From Frances F. Berdan and Patricia Rieff Anawalt, The Codex Mendoza, vol. 3 (Berkeley: University of California Press, 1992), p. 11.

The last page of the manuscript explains that it was intended to be sent to Spain. It somehow instead came into the possession of André Thevet, Henri II of France's royal cosmographer in the mid-sixteenth century, whose signature appears in several places on the manuscript.[16] In his own writings, Thevet did not hesitate to compare Mesoamerican pictographs and figures with Egyptian hieroglyphs, though he wavers in his terminology, recalling Legati's description of Chinese writing:

the Mexicans, in order to express their ideas, use characters resembling diverse terrestrial and aquatic animals and the head, feet, arms, and other limbs of a man, just as the Egyptians and Ethiopians did formerly in their hieroglyphic letters. Two such books I have by me, written by hand in the city of Themistitan, and filled with their characters and figures and the interpretations of them.[17]
When Thevet died, Richard Hakluyt acquired the manuscript, and upon his passing bequeathed it to Samuel Purchas, who published a woodcut reproduction of the opening illustration of the Codex Mendoza in his Hakluytus posthumus, or, Purchas his Pilgrimes (London, 1625) [Fig. 10].

Fig. 10. Samuel Purchas, Hakluytus posthumus, or, Purchas his Pilgrimes, vol. 3, p. 1068 (London: H. Fetherston, 1625). Original from the Library of Congress.

This image was in turn copied in a work that brings us full circle, back to the esotericism of Renaissance exegetes of emblemata, hieroglyphs, and Hermetic and Kabbalistic texts (as well as to the logic of Foucault's episteme founded on the principle of similitudes), with the figure of the Jesuit polymath Athanasius Kircher, the summum of that tradition as it had developed in the seventeenth century and perhaps also its last gasp.

Kircher included a copy of Purchas's woodcut of the Codex Mendoza in the last volume of his Oedipus Aegyptiacus (Rome, 1652–54) [Fig. 11].

Fig. 11. Athanasius Kircher, Oedipus Aegyptiacus, vol. 3 (Rome, 1652–54), p. 32. From Athanasius Kircher at Stanford.

This work was the culmination of Kircher's studies in ancient languages, presenting a fanciful and quasi-mystical interpretation of Egyptian hieroglyphics and a reaffirmation of his thesis that Egyptian writing (and wisdom and religion) provided the ancient foundations for that of the Indians, the East Asians, and even the Americans, however distorted and debased (in Kircher's understanding) that original writing and wisdom had become through the process of its dissemination in Antiquity. Or, as he put it in the work where he first formulated the thesis extensively, his Prodromus Coptus sive Aegyptiacus (Coptic or Egyptian Precursor, published in Rome in 1636), "Egypt was the nursery not only of the progress of the Christian religion in distant lands, but also of all the superstitions propagated to the world at every time."[18] For Kircher, in spreading the gospel of Christ throughout the world, the Apostles had simply followed the same routes taken by the priests of ancient Egypt in propagating their wisdom and writing to the rest of humanity beyond Europe. (link to Walsh essay on St. Thomas) Kircher's vision of language, history, and theology entails some fundamental affinity or similarity spanning a vast swath of lands and peoples, from North Africa, across Asia, and into the Americas: "India" writ large, in other words, from Vopel's "India Maior Aethiopiae" (Greater India of Ethiopia) in Africa, through his "India intra Gangem" (India before the Ganges) and "India extra Gangem" (India beyond the Ganges), all the way to the "India Orientalis" in "Asia Magna" (his Greater Asia, a label that appears in our North America) and the lands of the Mexica. Even though maps included in Kircher's publications depict a wide Pacific Ocean (or Mare Australe) and a strait between eastern Siberia and Alaska, conceptually at least these territories were contiguous.

Thus, while Mexican temples might lend themselves to easy comparison with Egyptian pyramids, as Kircher explicitly does in his discussion of the temple of Huitzilopochtli in the chapter of Oedipus Aegyptiacus titled "On the religion of the Americans, a parallel of the Egyptian" ("De religione Americanorum Aegyptiacae parallela") [Fig. 12], Asian pagodas do not.

Fig. 12. Pyramidal temple of "Horcholivos" (Huitzilopochtli) in Athanasius Kircher, Oedipus Aegyptiacus, vol. 1 (Rome: Vitale Mascardi, 1652–54), p. 422. Original from Universidad Complutense de Madrid,

Nevertheless, Kircher in fact describes pagodas as "pyramids" in his China illustrata (Amsterdam, 1667).[19] In the accompanying illustrations, furthermore, he distorts their representation, making them seem more "pyramidal," in order to assert their allegedly Egyptian origin and to bolster his claim that the roots of Chinese religion lay in Egypt [Fig. 13].[20]

Fig. 13. Colored engraving depicting a Chinese pagoda as a pyramid, from the edition of Kircher's China illustrata in Dutch, Toonneel van China (Amsterdam: Johannes Janssonius van Waesberge, 1668), following p. 164. Original from The Getty Research Institute.

Kircher makes a similar move in his treatment of Asian writing systems. In the series of encomia to Ferdinand III of Austria composed in dozens of different languages and scripts at the beginning of Oedipus Aegyptiacus, Kircher includes one purporting to be in Brahmi script, which he calls "hieroglyphic language of the Brahmans" ("lingua hieroglyphica Brachmanum"). And the origin of Chinese "hieroglyphic" characters ("characteres hieroglyphici Sinensium") is likewise Egyptian according to his China illustrata: "The ancient Chinese followed the Egyptians, from whom they are descended: they executed their writing not by a composition of letters but by figures constructed from various natural things," such as the characters he claims are derived from images of birds [Fig. 14].[21]

Fig. 14. Chinese characters derived from images of birds, in Athanasius Kircher, China monumentis qua sacris qua profanes illustrata (Amsterdam: Johannes Janssonius van Waesberge, 1667), p. 230. Original from The Getty Research Institute.

Kircher states his overarching thesis regarding the cultural genealogy of the peoples of the world who use non-alphabetic writing systems, or characteres significativi, at the beginning of this same section of China illustrata, titled "On Chinese Literature" (De Sinensium literatura): "it is certain that three nations, before all other peoples of the world, made use of these [characters], namely, the Chinese, the Brahmans, and the Mexicans."[22]

However much the accuracy of seventeenth-century maps had improved over Vopel's vision of the world, Kircher's works suggest that, in certain corners at least, the underlying principles behind Vopel's organization of geography remained in force. Asia and America might well have become clearly delineated continents, but they were part of a world in which the dream of recovering a universal, Adamic language—one that preserved a direct correspondence between words and things—was still conceivable, for its traces were retrievable in the visual echoes of the scripts of ancient and modern languages. This is the ethos evoked in an engraving by Zacharias Dolendo after a design by Karel van Mander printed in the last years of the sixteenth century depicting the confusion of tongues and peoples after work stopped on the Tower of Babel [Fig. 15].

Fig. 15. Zacharias Dolendo, after Karel van Mander, Confusio Babylonica (1597–1600). Original from Bibliothque municipal de Lyon.

In the foreground, groups of figures carry or hold three tablets, each bearing different alphabets, Hebrew, Greek, and Arabic. In the distance, hieroglyphics can be made out faintly on an obelisk [Fig. 16].

Fig. 16. Confusio Babylonica (detail).

Incongruously for a modern viewer, the group with the Arabic tablet are naked save for loincloths and headdresses; one wears the stereotypical East Indian or Amerindian feather headdress [Fig. 17].

Fig. 17. Confusio Babylonica (detail).

Is this an exercise in over-exuberant exoticism, in which truly no one is "at home," as Peter Mason might put it?[23] Or is it an image of literal confusion, with Amerindians carrying off an Arabic primer that will serve them no purpose—like the man in an Ottoman turban who seems to struggle at deciphering the Greek? Or perhaps they are the "Amerasian" children of Shem about to head eastward towards what will be their new home, Vopel's Asia Magna.
