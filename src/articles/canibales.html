Canibales

Vopel placed the label CANIBALES on his South American continent; just to the south of this label, we see a man chopping up a human body, and to the southeast of this image, the word Antropophagi. The Greek historian Herodotus perhaps first described the Androphagi as a mythical race of cannibals living north of Scythia, and indeed, directly north of Scythia on the map is an inscription that speaks of “Antropophagi.” In his Geography, Ptolemy wrote of an “Island of Cannibals" somewhere in the Bay of Bengal, and these islands are often labeled on Ptolemy’s Eleventh Map of Asia as the Insule Antropophagorum. races of Anthropophagi living in both the Maeandrus Mountains in the region of India Beyond the Ganges, as well as on islands in Southeast Asia called the Maniolae which are inhabited by cannibals called Manioli.[1] In the Middle Ages, John Mandeville also described cannibals on the islands of Southeast Asia.[2] As the historian Surekha Davies has discussed, in the Age of Encounters, cannibalism came to emblematize South America and in particular Brazil in the work of sixteenth century European historians and cosmographers.[3] While ethnographic and archaeological evidence does suggest the presence of cannibalistic activities in parts of the new world, the location of cannibalism in the Americas nevertheless also represented the work of Amerasian visions that regularly saw this Asian and Southeast Asian phenomenon in the Americas.
