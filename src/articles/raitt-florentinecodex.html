Another Indianness: Re-Imagining the Diversity of Figuration in the Florentine Codex

Louisa M. Raitt

The Florentine Codex, formally titled The General History of the Things of New Spain and named for the Laurentian Library of Florence in which it is housed, describes the lands, histories, and cultures of Aztec civilization.[1] The Spanish Franciscan Fray Bernardino de Sahagún (1499-1590) is credited with its compilation. Sahagún travelled to central Mexico in 1529, just eight years after Cortés' conquest of Tenochtitlan. As a missionary, he learned Nahuatl, enabling him to translate the psalms and the gospels for local populations. Sahagún's most notable accomplishment, however, was his assembling of this encyclopedic codex. However, the codex cannot be merely considered a work of Sahagún alone, for it was truly a verbal and visual documentation of Mexican civilization from an indigenous point of view. The text represents a monumental collaboration between indigenous and European populations in the first decades of contact between Europeans and Amerindians.[2]

The books of the codex are comprised of bilingual text, transliterated Nahuatl with side-by-side Spanish translation, accompanied by a series of complex and vividly colored images. The creation of such a complicated object depended on the Nahua tlacuilos, or scribe/painters, who were trained as Sahagún's students in the European style at the Colegio de Santa Cruz de Tlatelolco.[3] At times, the text offers not only translation but also contemporary interpretations of Aztec life, especially in moments when it contextualizes unfamiliar information for a European readership. The images, too, present a fusion of cultures. The Nahua tlacuilos who worked with Sahagún took great care to depict themselves in a way that retained the authenticity of their cultural traditions so that their culture could be preserved; at the same time, they depicted their culture in a European style in order to make it accessible and interesting to European audiences. In this way, the tlacuilos were active creators, Europeanizing their way of seeing while remaining faithful to ancient canons.[4] Serge Gruzinski called it an "unprecedented experiment," wherein the "Renaissance arts of sculpture, architecture and painting entered into a dialogue with traditions that had been cultivated by pre-Columbian civilizations for millennia."[5] How was this fascinating encyclopedic text received? How did its images settle into existing conceptualizations of the Indies and furthermore, how can these images be read as evidence of an Amerasian imaginary?

Philip II of Spain (1527-1598) believed that Sahagún's work was blasphemous. He mandated that Sahagún stop the book's production and in April of 1577, before the codex had been completed, ordered it to be sent to Madrid.[6] Despite the monarch's disapproval, it was likely used there almost immediately to teach Spanish missionaries about the Nahua people and their customs.[7] By 1580, the codex had arrived in the collection of the Medici family in Italy.[8] As Lia Markey has proposed, Philip II likely gifted the codex to Ferdinando I de' Medici (1549-1609) while he was a cardinal in Rome. As a custos, or superior, of the Franciscans, and as a man with a personal fascination for colonial objects, Ferdinando seemed to be the ideal candidate to guard the book.[9] When Ferdinando was appointed Grand Duke of Tuscany in 1587 and moved to Florence, he took the precious codex with him; it had clearly captured his attention and, as Markey's research has shown, greatly influenced the decoration of his Armeria by Ludovico Buti in the Galleria degli Uffizi.[10](On the Buti frescoes, see the essay by Margo Weitzman.)

During this age of expansion, objects, documents, and accounts of exploration "exposed Europe to even more distant peoples in Asia, Africa, and the Americas, which broke the boundaries of what Europeans knew about the world."[11] To Philip II and Ferdinando de Medici, this book would have represented yet another curiosity from the Indies. While they understood that the codex contained images of the Indians from the territory recently conquered by Cortés and in turn made available to mendicants, they were unable to comprehend how the book fit into a larger picture of world cultures. In simple terms, the codex offered its readers an abundance of information about this complex civilization and its images, while leaving open the question of what and where exactly the Indies were.

The codex used a great variety of representations of dress and costume to visualize Aztec conceptions of class, rank, and profession, as well as to distinguish between gods, mortals, and ixiptla (incarnated images).[12] Mapping such differences allowed indigenous artists to showcase the heterogeneity of the Aztec people.[13] While the European reception of these images was always beyond their control, as we shall see, these stylized figures perhaps inadvertently encouraged Europeans to understand the Indies as lands where America and Asia overlapped. (On the range of meanings of the word India and Indians, see the entry by Vittoria Riccio.) The codex emphasizes variations in things like costume and skin color as markers of difference, a point made clearly by two groupings of figures in particular: the portraits of the Aztec deities in book one and the artists' portraits in book nine. These two sets of figures assimilate the Aztec into the category of the exotic, setting up the possibility for their conflation with other Indians from other distant places.

Book one employs a sequence of full-page illustrations of the personifications of the Aztec gods (Fig. 1).

Fig. 1: Fray Bernardino de Sahagún and Nahua artists, Aztec Deities, the Florentine Codex. ca. 1555-1577. Florence, Biblioteca Medicea Laurenziana, Mediceo Palatino 218, Book I, 14r-16r.

Each page is divided into two registers and further subdivided, making equal rectangular frames to house four different Aztec gods per page. The gods vary in size, orientation, and manifest slight variations in posture; however most are shown in profile holding a scepter in one hand and a shield or other militaristic or ceremonial device in the other. The most striking differences are in the vestments worn by each deity; some don full feathered capes, others simple, short, skirts, while others still are mostly clothed with long tunics over long skirts. They also possess a wide variety of headgear, some bearing feather headdresses, others ornate crowns, and some no headwear at all. The skin tones of the gods, too, run a wide spectrum, from dark black figures to others with skin colored bright red or yellow in hue. Some have skin that is whiter than the background of the page. While the Nahua may have hoped to use these images to represent and preserve the complexity of Aztec culture, this array of skin tones would have likely registered as an "ethnographic map" for a European audience, a map that worked to categorize the people of the Indies by different types rather than to underscore the nuanced variety of this civilization.[14] While race was not an established social construct in the sixteenth century, many proto-ethnographic accounts from the far reaches of the world written during this period took special note of skin color, catering to an audience keenly attuned to nuances in pigment and physiognomy.[15] Seeing these diverse exotic figures in the pages of the codex could easily have suggested to European readers that these people inhabited a part of the world with a diversity of climates and cultures: the Indies, or as an extension of India.

The codex's emphasis on diversity and its suggestion of the significant geographic range of Aztec peoples stands in stark contrast to the flattened portrayal of the Amerindian in the illustrations of the Trachtenbuch of Christoph Weiditz from some decades earlier (Fig. 2).

Fig. 2: Christoph Weiditz (German), Trachtenbuch, Germanische Nationalmuseum, Nuremberg, Hs. 22474.4. Top, pg. 12–13; Middle, pg. 6-7; Bottom, pg. 2-3.

The Trachtenbuch contained not only full page illustrations of European traditional costume but also included portraits of New World peoples brought back to Spain in 1528 by Hernán Cortés. Drawing our attention to the thirteen paintings of Amerindians in the Trachtenbuch, Elizabeth Boone points out that it was difficult "for Europeans then—and even for scholars until recently—to recognize real ethnic, cultural, and, indeed, social distinctions among the indigenous people of the Americas." By contrast, "how easy it was simply to blend them together as exotics." [16] As the glosses of the Trachtenbuch suggest, the Indies were simply distant lands. Therefore, the intent of Nahua artists to depict the Aztecs as a complex and heterogeneous culture was likely lost on European audiences; what stuck, perhaps, was more the vibrancy and exoticism that worked to confirm European perceptions of Indianness.

The presence and absence of clothing also played an important role in Europeans' understanding of the Indians. European chroniclers typically portrayed the conquest of the Aztecs through scenes of armored Spanish soldiers defeating a population of naked or nearly naked indigenous figures. The art historian Karel Van Mander, for instance, describes such a scene painted by the Dutch artist Jan Mostaert as "a landscape, a West Indian one, with many naked people." (Fig. 3) [17]

Fig. 3: Jan Mostaert (Dutch), Landscape with an Episode from the Conquest of America, ca. 1535. Rijksmuseum.

Their nakedness, along with their primitive technology and contrasting skin tone were meant to register both a geospatial and temporal distance. And yet, this is not how other types of Indians are portrayed. Images of other civilizations that made up the Indies, what we would call Asia for example, did not often employ depictions of indigenous peoples as naked or near-naked.[18] In fact, when members of Ferdinando de Medici's court saw Jacopo Zucchi's Allegory of the Americas (Fig. 4), they understood the figures in the foreground to be "beautiful Roman women" that were disguised as "New World Natives."[19] Perhaps his remarks indicate that nudity had come to be synonymous with the Americas, while diverse clothing signaled something else entirely.[20]

Fig. 4: Jacopo Zucchi (Italian), Allegory of the Americas, ca. 1590. Lviv National Art Gallery.

A related trope, the allegories of the continents, which became increasingly popular in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, reveals a similar tension between nude and clothed figures. America (along with Africa) is nearly always personified as a naked figure, adorned with beautiful jewels but left unclothed and vulnerable. The personification of Asia, on the other hand, is dressed in fine garments; while still foreign and exotic, it was usually associated with Europe through clothing.[21]

Figs. 5-8: Adriaen Collaert (Netherlandish), The Four Continents, n.d. Engravings. Each 21.3 x 25.6 cm. Fig. 5: America. Fig. 6: Asia. Fig. 7: Europa. Fig. 8: Africa

A sixteenth century engraved series personifying the continents by Adriaen Collaert (Figs. 5-8) portrays America (Fig. 5) as a woman, scantily clad with a swatch of drapery gently covering her lap, leaving her torso entirely exposed. Her curly hair is tucked under a feather-plumed headdress and she grasps a bow and arrow. Alternatively, his personification of Asia (Fig. 6) wears sandals and a complicated costume, resembling the armored dress and robes of a militant saint or angel in the Christian tradition. She swings an incense burner and dons a headdress, which billows in the wind behind her. Furthermore, even if her eyes do not meet ours, Asia is facing forward toward the viewer, much like Europa (Fig. 7); America, on the other hand, looks back behind her shoulder into the distance beyond, where the Amerindians suffer losses in battle against invading European forces.

European allegories of Asia reinforce Marco Polo's representations of Cathay, and of Asia as a prosperous ancient civilization. Even Francis Xavier, upon his arrival to Japan in the mid-sixteenth century, remarked that the inhabitants were pale skinned like Europeans; for Xavier, this must have been because Japan shares the same latitude as Italy, for "the climate of Japan resembles that of Italy, and the natives are [also] much like the Italians in height."[22] Since the time of Marco Polo, Europeans saw civility and wealth in the Orient; knowing this, we can imagine that the wide array of intricate garments depicted on the pages of the Florentine Codex could have easily registered as Asian for learned European audiences.

Details in the artist portraits in book nine have similar implications (Fig. 9).

Fig. 9: Fray Bernardino de Sahagœn and Nahua artists, The Art of the Amanteca (feather artists), the Florentine Codex, ca. 1555-1577. Florence, Biblioteca Medicea Laurenziana, Mediceo Palatino 219 Book IX, chapter 21, fol. 65v and 66r.

Here, both text and image represent the production of a variety of Aztec crafts, including work in charcoal, clay, glue, gold, gems, and feathers. The portraits of the artists serve several functions in the text. Firstly, they highlight the artists' embrace of European artistic techniques learned in their training in the newly established Franciscan schools. The painter is attentive to situate the amantecas, or Aztec featherworkers for example, in a typical Renaissance architectural setting, incorporating doorways flanked by columns, corbelled archways, receding tile floors, background landscapes, and even adjusting the positioning of the figures all to reveal the use of linear perspective, a technique not used prior to Spanish conquest. Indigenous artists clearly used the codex to flaunt their quick mastery of European techniques, but in a way that translated traditional artistic practices into a format legible to European audiences. Secondly, each vignette is different in its architectural details and setting. The diversity of wardrobe and skin color emphasized in book one extends here to a variety of physical differences among the individuals depicted. As Magaloni Kerpel has shown, the Nahua painted these artists as careful portraits; no two faces are the same.[23] These portraits thus represent both the desire of the Nahua to display their individuality and diversity in a style that would resonate with European readers.

And yet, this clothing is far from Amerindian. While the amantecas work on feather crafts, for instance, they wear draped grey tunics and resemble portraits of medieval clerical artists more than Amerindians from the Trachtenbuch. These garments would be familiar to a late sixteenth-century European eye; and yet, when worn by new world peoples, the viewer is asked to consider the sophistication of the civilization from which these Indians and their objects hail. If they dress like us, and look like us—or as allegorical images suggest, if they wear clothing, as did Europeans and Asians —then they too could be like the natives of the marvelous and sophisticated lands of the East.

A heightened attention to individuality and difference, the contextualization of Aztec culture in Renaissance settings, the application of European artistic techniques, and the portrayal of these artists in professional European costume all worked to unsettle images of the Amerindian and detached the Nahua from any particular place and time. Instead, these images left room for the European viewer to envisage these figures occupying a multitude of different lands in the Indies. Indeed, Ferdinando de Medici acquired the codex not because it offered insight into a particular history of a particular civilization, but rather because he saw it as "a valuable 'rarity', adding this work of art to his own [growing] collection of [other] exotica in Florence."[24]

Perhaps the diverse array of figures displayed in the Florentine Codex represented a kind of contact zone, or a "zone of convergence where East and West met and crossed over into one another."[25] Furthermore, it signified an ambiguous place where the barbarous New World filled with luxurious gems, feathers, gold and silver, and nude savage figures fell into and mixed with the opulent known richness of the east, the luxury goods – the gold, the porcelain, and the silks – that had already come to European courts from the East. To be clear, much of this discussion remains speculative; we have no direct testimony on the part of Europeans declaring how, exactly, they understood the images in Sahagún's compilation, or to what other representations they may have likened them. Nevertheless, clearly, there was clearly potential for European readers to identify the figures in the codex as Indian, broadly cast, or as Asian.

Vopel's map echoes this ambiguity. "Temixtitan" (another spelling of Tenochtitlan) appears in capital letters just to the south of the cartographic sign for the city, a castle with four spires cast out in the middle of a lake, and Vopel places the Nahua just to the northeast.[26] Nearby "Atacuba" (Tacuba) marks the site of the pre-Hispanic Nahua city of Tlacopan, meaning "place of the jarilla plant". Just to the northwest of "Nautal" on the map is "Leztalap" which could be a poor transliteration of the Ancient Aztec city of Iztapalapa. Further north lies the city of 'Gualncingo' (Tulancingo), the place from which Prince Ixtlilxochitl gathered an army here to join Hernán Cortés to conquer Tenochtitlan in 1521. All of these places in the valley of Mexico are just northwest of Tampin, a city in Malaysia that lies on the border of Malacca and just south of Singur, a known city on the Indian subcontinent. In effect, Vopel's map suggests that the Florentine Codex, made in Tlatelolco, was produced in the lands where East and West overlapped.

In these ways, the Florentine Codex was an open text, capable of being mobilized within a European worldview that was itself also under development. A later sixteenth-century edition of the Florentine Codex that has recently been discovered at the Hispanic Society of America in New York, with text translated into Italian and the cover bearing Cardinal Ferdinando's coat of arms, also highlights the codex's flexible, Amerasian nature.[27] This copy, likely made in the 1580s, lacks images, but "has spaces intended for images... [which] correspond precisely to the location of the images in the Florentine Codex."[28] It proves Ferdinando's contemporary fascination with the codex and continues to captivate our imagination now. Could we have better understood the way in which the codex was received if Ferdinando's artists had finished their version, copying and perhaps refashioning not only the text but the images as well? What would these new world images have become in the hands of European artists? And more precisely, what version of Indianness would these artists have expressed?
