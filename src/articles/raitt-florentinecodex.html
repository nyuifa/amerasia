Another Indianness: Re-Imagining the Diversity of Figuration in the Florentine Codex

Louisa M. Raitt

The Florentine Codex, named for the Laurentian Library of Florence in which it is housed, is formally titled The General History of the Things of New Spain, and is divided into twelve sections.[1] Each of these narratives delves deeply into the description of the native lands, histories, and cultures of the Aztec civilization as encountered by the Spanish during the first decades of contact. A Spanish Franciscan, Fray Bernardino de Sahagún (1499-1590) is credited with the compilation of the Codex. Sahagún travelled to central Mexico in 1529, just eight years after Cortés’ conquest of the Aztec Empire. He was primarily devoted to his missionary task,learning the local language of Nahuatl so that he could translate the psalms and the gospels into the native tongue. However, his most notable accomplishment was assembling the encyclopedic Florentine Codex, which documented the ‘New World’ from an indigenous worldview. The text, however, cannot merely be considered a work of Sahagún alone; for it is truly a verbal and visual construction of the Amerindian based on direct contribution from the Nahua people, marking an important collaboration between native and European populations in the New World.[2]

The books of the codex are comprised of bilingual text, transliterated Nahuatl with an accompanying Spanish translation, and a set of complex and vividly colored images. The execution of such a complicated object depended on the Nahua tlacuilos who, as both scribes and painters, were trained as Sahagún’s students at the Colegio de Santa Cruz de Tlatelolco in the European style.[3] At times, the Spanish translation of the original Nahuatl text offers not only a simple translation but also a contemporary interpretation, contextualizing and cushioning the unfamiliar information for a European readership. The images, too, present a fusion of cultures. Serge Gruzinski called it an “unprecedented experiment,” wherein the “Renaissance arts of sculpture, architecture and painting entered into a dialogue with traditions that had been cultivated by pre-Columbian civilizations for millennia.”[4] In its collaborative nature, The Florentine Codex includes the indigenous perspective directly in the image-making, image-explaining, and image-viewing process during the age of encounter between Europe and the Indies.  How was this fascinating encyclopedic text received? How did its images settle into existing conceptualizations of the Indies and furthermore, how can these images be read as models of Amerasian imaginary?

The Nahua tlacuilos took great care to depict themselves in a way that retained the authenticity of their cultural traditions so that their culture could be preserved; at the same time, their work embodies and employs traditions of the Renaissance avant-garde so their culture may be seen and understood as a valuable contributor in a larger artistic discourse. The tlacuilos were active creators, Europeanizing their way of seeing while remaining faithful to ancient canons.[5] The Codex may be read as a project of self-representation, using dress and costume to determine visual indicators of class, rank, and profession and also to distinguish gods from mortals from ixiptla (incarnated images).[6] Mapping their difference, they were exemplifying the heterogeneity of the Aztec people.[7] However, the reception of their efforts extended far beyond their control. Through their individualistic and highly stylized figurations, the Nahua tlacuilos complicated and thus enabled existing European approaches to understanding the Indies, playing into visual models for both America and Asia. (On the range of meanings of the word India and Indians, see the entry by Vittoria Riccio.)

In April of 1577, before the Codex had been completed, Philip II of Spain (1527-1598) mandated that Sahagún stop all book production considering the works to be blasphemous in nature and ordered that the Codex be sent to himin Madrid.[8] When it arrived, it was likely initially used to teach Spanish missionaries about the Nahua people and customs.[9] However, by 1580, the Codex had arrived in the collection of the Medici family in Italy.[10] As Lia Markey has proposed, the Codex was likely gifted by Philip II to Ferdinando I de' Medici (1549-1609) while he was a cardinal in Rome. As guardian of the Franciscan friars, and as a man with a personal fascination for colonial objects, he seemed to be the ideal candidate to guard the book.[11] When Ferdinando was appointed Grand Duke of Tuscany in 1587 and moved to Florence, he took his precious Codex with him. The Codex clearly captivated Ferdinando’s attention and, as Markey’s research has shown, greatly influenced the decoration of his Armeria by Ludovico Buti in the Galleria degli Uffizi.[12] (On the Buti frescoes, see the essay Margo Weitzman.)

            During this age of expansion, objects, documents, and accounts of exploration “exposed Europe to even more distant peoples in Asia, Africa, and the Americas, which broke the boundaries of what Europeans knew about the world.”[13] To Philip II and Ferdinando de Medici this book would have represented yet another souvenir or curiosity from that world, the new world, the Indies that seemed to be ever expanding. While they understood that the Codex boasted images of the Indians from the territory recently conquered by Cortés and made available to the missionary work of the Mendicant orders, they were unable to comprehend with exactitude where the book fit into the larger picture of world cultures. In simple terms, the Codex offered its readers an abundance of information about this complex civilization and its images, while leaving open the question of its relation to the larger Indian extension.

            The codex emphasizes variations of costume, skin color, and individuality as primary markers of difference, a point made clearly by two groupings of figures: the portraits of the Aztec deities of book one and the artists’ portraits of book nine.[14] These two sets of figures assimilate the Aztec into the category of the exotic, allowing for the conflation of the Amerindian with other Indians from other distant places.

            Book one employs a sequence of full-page illustrations of the personifications of the Aztec gods (Fig. 1). Each page is divided into two registers and further subdivided making equal rectangular frames to house four different Aztec gods per page. The gods vary in size, orientation, and manifest slight variations in posture; however most are shown in profile holding a scepter in one hand and a shield or other militaristic or ceremonial device in the other. The most striking differences are in the vestments worn by each deity: some don full feathered capes, others simple, short, skirts, while others still are mostly clothed with long tunics over long skirts. They also possess a wide variety of headgear, some bearing feather headdresses, others ornate crowns, and some no headwear at all. The skin tones of the gods, too, run a wide spectrum, from dark black figures to others with skin colored bright red or yellow in hue, and even to skin more white than the background of the page. While possibly representative of the intentions of the Nahua to represent the heterogeneity and preserve the cultural variations of the Aztec people, this array of skin tones certainly would have registered as an “ethnographic map” or chart of diversity for a European audience that served to categorize the people who inhabited the Indies by their different types rather than act as portraits of nuanced variety within a single civilization.[15]  While race was not a formal social construct in the sixteenth century, many proto-ethnographic accounts from the far reaches of the world written during this period took special note of skin color, catering to an audience keenly attuned to nuances in pigment and physiognomy.[16] Seeing these diverse exotic figures occupying the same pages of a single book would have suggested that they inhabited a part of the world with a diversity of climates and cultures, reinforcing the idea of a vast Indian extension.

            The emphasis on diversity, with its suggestion of significant geographic range, stands in contrast to the flattened portrayal of the Amerindian in the illustrations of the Trachtenbuch of Christoph Weiditz from some decades earlier (Fig. 3). The Trachtenbuch contained not only full page illustrations of European traditional costume but also included portraits of natives brought back to Spain in 1528 by Hernando Cortés to join the court of Charles V. Drawing our attention to the thirteen paintings of Amerindians in the Trachtenbuch, Elizabeth Boone points out that it was difficult “for Europeans then—and even for scholars until recently—to recognize real ethnic, cultural, and, indeed, social distinctions among the indigenous people of the Americas.” By contrast, “how easy it was simply to blend them together as exotics.”[17] The understanding of the Indies, as revealed by the glosses of the Trachtenbuch, is simply a land far away, with gold in the water, the land of the Indians. Therefore, the intent of the Nahua artists to showcase the Aztec as a multivalent people comprised of many heterogeneous cultures is lost on the receiving European audience, and what sticks is the vibrancy and exoticism that validate a European perception of Indianness.

            The representation of clothing, and its absence, plays another important role for the European imaginary. The conquest of the Aztec was often portrayed as armored Spanish soldiers dominating a population of naked, or nearly naked, barbarous figures. A sixteenth-century painting by the Dutch artist Jan Mostaert (Fig. 4), for example, is described by Karel Van Mander as “a landscape, a West Indian one, with many naked people.”[18] Their nakedness, along with their basic technology and contrasting skin tone were meant to register both a geospatial and temporal distance. And yet, this is not how other types of Indians are portrayed. Images of other civilizations that made up the Indies, what we would call Asia for example, did not often warrant naked or near-naked depictions.[19] In fact, when members of Ferdinando de Medici’s court saw Jacopo Zucchi’s Allegory of the Americas (Fig. 5), they understood the figures in the foreground to be “beautiful Roman woman” that were disguised as “New World Natives.”[20] Perhaps his remarks indicate that nudity had come to be synonymous with America; whereas complex codified difference based on diverse clothing signaled something else entirely.

            A related trope, the allegories of the continents, popularized during the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries reveals a similar tension between nude and clothed figures. America (along with Africa) is nearly always personified as a naked figure, adorned with beautiful jewels but left unclothed and vulnerable. Asia on the other hand, much like Europe, is dressed in fine garments; while still very foreign and exotic to the European eye, the personification of Asia is seen as dignified merely by its clothed association with Europe.[21] A sixteenth century engraved series personifying the continents by Adriaen Collaert (Fig. 7) portrays America as a woman, scantily clad with a swatch of drapery gently covering her lap, leaving her torso entirely exposed. Her curly hair is tucked under a feather-plumed headdress and she grasps a bow and arrow. Alternatively, his personification for Asia wears sandals and a complicated costume, resembling the armored dress robes of militant saint or angel in the Christian tradition. She swings an incense burner and dons a headdress, which billows in the wind behind her. Furthermore, even if her eyes do not meet ours Asia is facing forward toward the viewer, offering another point of similitude with the engraved Europa; America on the other hand, looks back behind her shoulder into the distance beyond, where the Amerindians are suffering loss in battle against the invading European forces. These examples together reinforce the imagery that was created by Marco Polo’s opulent descriptions from his journey to the wondrous and civilized Cathay, the land of the Great Khan.[22] This imagery persisted in the mindset of other European explorers and the association of Asia as a land of a prosperous ancient civilization, not a barbarous and savage unknown, endured for centuries. Even Francis Xavier, upon his arrival to Japan in the mid sixteenth century, remarked that the inhabitants were pale skinned, like Europeans and that it must be because Japan shares the same latitude as Italy, for “the climate of Japan resembles that of Italy, and the natives are [also] much like the Italians in height.”[23] Since the time of Marco Polo, Europeans saw civility and wealth in the lands to the east, the lands of Asia; perhaps then we can image that the sheer presence and wide array of intricate garments depicted on the pages of the Florentine Codex thus registered the Indians therein as part of an equally sophisticated, if not the same fascinating civilization.

            A similar argument can be woven through a discussion of the artist portraits in book nine which offer a heightened degree of detail, attention to individual difference, and the careful representation of the sophisticated visual culture of the Nahua artists. The pages, through both text and image, display various crafts including charcoal and clay work, glue making, gold making, lapidary, and feather making. The portraits of the artists serve several functions within the book. Firstly, they reflect the artists’ incorporation of stylistic qualities learned through their training in the newly established Franciscan schools. The painter is attentive to situate the amantecas, or featherworkers for example, in a typical Renaissance architectural setting incorporating doorways flanked by columns, corbelled archways, receding tile floors, background landscapes, and even adjusting the positioning of the figures all to reveal the use of linear perspective, a technique not used prior to Spanish conquest. The painters clearly used the codex as an opportunity to flaunt their quick mastery of European techniques but in a way that translates their traditional artistic practices into a visually legible format for European audiences.

Secondly, each vignette is used to highlight individuality and difference. For example, in each vignette, the architectural details change and no two settings are the same. The individuality and diversity of wardrobe and skin color emphasized in book one, previously discussed, extends her to physical difference on an individual level. As seen in a detail from Magaloni Kerpel’s book The Colors of the New World,[24] the Nahua painted the artists as careful portraits; no two faces are the same. These portraits thus represent both the desire of the Nahua to display their own individuality and diversity in a mode of visuality that would register with their European readership.

            And yet, the clothing system used in these artist portraits distances them from the stereotypical Amerindian imagery. While the amantecas work on feather crafts, they wear draped grey tunics and resemble portraits of medieval clerical artists more closely than the Amerindians from the Trachtenbuch. These garments would be familiar to a late sixteenth century European eye; and yet, when applied to these artist figures and cast into an Amerindian world, the viewer is asked to consider the civility and sophistication of the civilization from which these Indians and their objects hail. If they dress like us, and look like us, then they too could be like the natives of the lands to the East, the sophisticated and wealthy Indies where the gold runs in the water and where the Great Kahn made his home.

            The heightened attention to individuality and difference, the contextualization in Renaissance settings, the application of European artistic techniques, and the portrayal of these artists in professional systematized costume troubled the understanding of the Amerindian and detached the Nahua from any particular place and time. Instead, these images left room for the European viewer to envisage these figures occupying a multitude of different lands, many of which they had conflated or overlapped in their working imaginary of the Indies. Ferdinando de Medici acquired the book not because it offered insight into a particular history of a particular civilization but rather because he saw it as “a valuable ‘rarity’, adding this work of art to his own [growing] collection of [other] exotica in Florence.”[25]

            Perhaps the diverse array of figures displayed in the Florentine Codex represented a sort of alpha and omega,[26] a “zone of convergence where East and West met and crossed over into one another,”[27] Furthermore, it signified an ambiguous place where the barbarous new world filled of luxurious gems, feathers, gold and silver, and nude savage figures fell into and mixed with the opulent known richness of the east, the luxury goods - the gold, the porcelain, and the silks – that had already come to European courts from the East.

            This ambiguity can be seen on Caspar Vopel’s map printed by Vavassore in 1588 which details a multitude of sites from the Mexican valley amidst known Asian locales. ‘Temixtitan’ (another spelling of Tenochtitlan) appears in capital letters, just to the south of the cartographic sign for the city, a castle with four spires cast out in the middle of a lake.[28] The Nahua specifically are included by Vopel, situated within this cartographic environ, just above and to the northeast of the Temixtitan label, as indicated by the pin of this entry. Additionally, nearby, ‘Atacuba’ (Tacuba) marks the site of the pre-Hispanic Nahua city of Tlacopan, meaning place of the jarilla plant. Just to the northwest of ‘Nautal’ on the map is ‘Leztalap’ which could be a poor transliteration of the Ancient Aztec city of Iztapalapa. Further north lies the city of ‘Gualncingo’ (Tulancingo), the place from which Prince Ixtlilxochitl gathered an army here to join Hernán Cortés to conquer Tenochtitlan in 1521. However, what complicates this further is that these places of the valley of Mexico are just northwest of Tampin, a city in Malaysia that lies on the border of Malacca and just south of Singur, a known city on the Indian subcontinent. As a result, the location of the site of production of the Florentine Codex as noted in the Vopel map represents the physical conflation or confusion of East and West which some viewers would have projected onto the visual imagery of its pages. 

            In these ways, the Florentine Codex was an open text, capable of being mobilized into a worldview that was itself under development. Its open-ended reception is made evident by the partial copy of The Florentine Codex that has recently been discovered at the Hispanic Society of America in New York, with the text translated into Italian and the cover bearing Cardinal Ferdinando’s coat of arms.[29] This copy, likely made in the 1580s, “lacks images,” but “has spaces intended for images… [which] correspond precisely to the location of the images in the Florentine Codex.”[30] It proves Ferdinando’s contemporary fascination with the Codex and also captivates our imagination now. Could we have better understood the way in which the Codex was received if Ferdinando’s artists had finished their version, translating not only the text but the images as well? What would these new world images have become in the hands of European artists? And more precisely, what version of Indianness would have inhabited their imagined world?
